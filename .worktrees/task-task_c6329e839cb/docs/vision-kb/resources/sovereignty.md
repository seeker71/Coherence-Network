# Sovereignty — What the Field Generates from Itself

> Everything the field needs, the field can generate, harvest, grow, build, ferment,
> weave, share, or compost. What it cannot generate, it circulates with the world
> around it. There is no scarcity in a living system — only flow.
>
> *The internal-resources companion to this room's external resources. Where [aligned-communities.md](aligned-communities.md), [new-earth-integration.md](new-earth-integration.md), and the open-source plans in [INDEX.md](INDEX.md) hold what we connect to outside, this file holds what the field produces from within: eight domains of sovereignty (Energy, Water, Food, Building Materials, Knowledge Circulation, Creative, External Interface, Waste-as-Compost) with specific quantities, technologies, and costs for a community of ~100 cells. A practical manual someone building a field could study end-to-end.*

## 1. Energy Sovereignty

### What a Community of 100 Actually Needs

The average American household consumes 10,500 kWh per year. A household of 2.5 people
means roughly 4,200 kWh per person per year. A community of 100 in the conventional
model would consume approximately 420,000 kWh annually.

The field consumes a fraction of this. Here is why:

- **No individual heating or cooling.** Shared thermal mass, passive solar, and the
  mass rocket stove eliminate 40% of conventional energy use.
- **No individual cooking.** One communal hearth replaces 40 separate kitchens. A mass
  rocket stove uses 80-90% less fuel than an open fire and 50-70% less than a
  conventional stove.
- **No clothes dryers.** Line drying. Wind and sun are free.
- **No individual refrigeration.** Root cellars, fermentation, seasonal eating, and
  a small number of shared cold-storage units replace 40 refrigerators.
- **Minimal lighting.** Structures designed for natural light. Evening is firelight
  and candlelight with targeted solar-powered LEDs where needed.
- **No commuting.** No cars. No fuel. The field is walkable.
- **Shared technology.** A tech nook with shared computers replaces 40 individual
  setups running 24/7.

**Realistic estimate for 100 cells:** 30,000-50,000 kWh per year. That is less energy
than 5 average American homes. Less than 10 in most European countries.

### Solar

A single 400W solar panel produces approximately 500-700 kWh per year depending on
location (1,200-1,800 peak sun hours). Cost: $200-400 per panel in 2024.

For 40,000 kWh/year: 60-80 panels. Total array: approximately 30-40 kW.

**Cost:** $12,000-32,000 for panels. Inverters, racking, wiring: another $8,000-15,000.
Total installed: $20,000-47,000 for DIY community installation (commercial installation
would be $60,000-120,000, but the field builds its own).

**Over 25 years:** That 30 kW array produces 750,000-1,050,000 kWh. At even $0.10/kWh
(low), that is $75,000-105,000 of energy from a $20,000-47,000 investment. The panels
pay for themselves in 5-8 years, then produce free energy for 17-20 more.

**Real example:** Tamera Solar Village in Portugal runs a 100kW solar concentrator
(the Scheffler reflector design) that generates steam for cooking, sterilization, and
electricity for their community of 170 people. Cost was a fraction of conventional
infrastructure. The technology is open-source.

**Real example:** Auroville generates much of its electricity from rooftop solar across
its community structures, supplemented by wind. Their Solar Kitchen serves 1,000 meals
a day using a solar bowl concentrator — a 15-meter diameter parabolic reflector that
focuses sunlight to generate steam for cooking.

### Wind

A small wind turbine (5-10 kW) produces 8,000-15,000 kWh/year in a good wind site
(average wind speed above 5 m/s). Cost: $15,000-30,000 installed.

Wind is site-dependent. It complements solar perfectly — wind tends to be strongest in
winter and at night, when solar is weakest. A hybrid system provides more consistent
generation.

### Micro-Hydro

If the land has a stream with even modest flow and head (vertical drop), micro-hydro
is the most consistent renewable source. A stream flowing at 10 liters/second with
10 meters of head generates approximately 700W continuous — that is 6,100 kWh/year,
24 hours a day, 365 days a year.

Cost: $2,000-8,000 for a micro-hydro system. No batteries needed if the stream runs
year-round — it IS the battery.

**Real example:** Many Earthship communities in New Mexico supplement solar with
small-scale hydro where available. In the mountains of Colombia, Gaviotas developed
micro-hydro systems that power their entire community from streams that were restored
by their reforestation work.

### Biomass and Biogas

Kitchen scraps, animal manure, and crop residue from a community of 100 generate
substantial biogas potential. A simple anaerobic digester processing the waste from
100 people plus animal manure produces 2-5 cubic meters of biogas per day — enough
for 2-4 hours of cooking or significant supplemental heating.

Cost: A community-scale biogas digester can be built for $1,000-5,000 using local
materials. The Chinese fixed-dome design and the Indian floating-drum design are both
proven at this scale and thoroughly documented.

The digestate (what comes out) is excellent fertilizer — the loop closes.

### The Mass Rocket Stove — The Field's Heart

This is the single most important energy technology in the field. A mass rocket stove
is not a conventional woodstove. It is a combustion system that:

- Burns wood at 1,000+ degrees Celsius in a small, insulated combustion chamber
- Captures 80-90% of the heat energy (conventional fireplaces: 10-15%)
- Routes hot exhaust gases through a thermal mass — a cob bench, an earthen floor,
  a wall — that absorbs and radiates heat for 12-24 hours after the fire goes out
- Produces almost no smoke (the combustion is so complete that exhaust is mostly
  CO2 and water vapor)
- Burns small-diameter wood, branches, and scrap — not logs

**What one mass rocket stove provides:**

- **Heating** for the entire hearth space (50-100 square meters) from a thermal mass
  bench and floor. In a well-insulated cob structure, one 2-hour burn in the morning
  keeps the space warm all day. A second evening burn keeps it warm all night.
- **Cooking** via a flat cooktop surface over the combustion chamber. Multiple pots,
  bread baking, water heating — all from the same fire.
- **Hot water** via a copper coil run through the thermal mass or the chimney,
  feeding an insulated tank. 200-400 liters of hot water per day from the same fire
  that heats the space and cooks the food.
- **Gathering** — the warm bench IS the social infrastructure. In winter, the whole
  field gravitates to the rocket stove bench. It is the warmest, most comfortable,
  most social place in the community. Elders claim their spot. Children fall asleep
  on the warm earth. Stories are told against the radiant heat.

**Wood consumption:** A mass rocket stove heating a community hearth and cooking for
100 people uses approximately 3-5 tons of wood per year. That is less than a single
conventional woodstove heating one American home (5-8 tons/year). A managed half-hectare
woodlot coppiced on a 7-year rotation produces 3-5 tons of small-diameter wood annually,
in perpetuity.

**Cost to build:** $200-500 in materials (firebrick for the combustion chamber, clay,
sand, straw, a steel barrel for the heat riser, stovepipe). Built by the community in
a week. Ianto Evans and Leslie Jackson documented the entire process in "Rocket Mass
Heaters" — the book costs $25 and contains everything needed.

**Real example:** The Cob Cottage Company in Oregon has built dozens of mass rocket
stoves in community settings. Aprovecho Research Center tested and optimized the
design over decades. Thousands have been built worldwide.

### Human Energy

Not metaphorical. A human body at moderate work output produces 75-100 watts of
sustained mechanical energy. 20 cells working together on a building project for
4 hours produce the equivalent of 6-8 kWh — enough to run a power tool for a day.

The field's primary energy source IS human bodies fueled by food grown on the land.
Building, digging, carrying, mixing cob, harvesting, processing — this is energy
conversion from solar (via plants, via food) to mechanical work. No extraction.
No transmission loss. No grid. The most elegant energy system is a well-fed human
doing work that makes them more alive.

### Battery Storage

For the solar/wind system, battery storage provides energy when the sun is not shining
and wind is not blowing. A Tesla Powerwall stores 13.5 kWh and costs $8,000-12,000.
For a community of 100, 2-4 days of storage (60,000-160,000 kWh... this is where
it gets expensive).

**Practical approach:** Size the battery for 1-2 days of essential loads only
(water pumps, communication, cold storage, minimal lighting): 30-50 kWh of storage.
Cost: $15,000-30,000 for lithium-ion. Lead-acid batteries are cheaper ($8,000-15,000)
but shorter-lived.

**Alternative:** If micro-hydro is available, it provides baseload and batteries are
barely needed. If biomass/biogas is available, a generator can fill gaps. The field
designs its energy system to minimize battery dependence through:

- Thermal mass (stores heat energy in earth and stone — free, infinite cycles)
- Root cellars (stores cooling energy underground — free)
- Fermentation (stores food energy in microbial cultures — free)
- Scheduling high-energy activities for sunny/windy periods

### The Energy Portrait

A community of 100 cells, fully energy-sovereign:

| System | Capital cost | Annual output | Lifespan |
|--------|-------------|---------------|----------|
| Solar array (30 kW) | $20,000-47,000 | 36,000-42,000 kWh | 25-30 years |
| Small wind (5 kW) | $15,000-30,000 | 8,000-15,000 kWh | 20 years |
| Micro-hydro (if available) | $2,000-8,000 | 6,000+ kWh | 30+ years |
| Biogas digester | $1,000-5,000 | cooking gas equiv. | 20+ years |
| Battery storage (40 kWh) | $15,000-25,000 | (storage) | 10-15 years |
| Mass rocket stove (x2) | $400-1,000 | all heating + cooking + hot water | 20+ years with maintenance |
| **Total** | **$53,400-116,000** | **50,000+ kWh + all thermal** | — |

That is $534-1,160 per person for full energy sovereignty. Compare: the average
American pays $1,500/year for energy. The field's entire energy infrastructure costs
less than one year of energy bills for 100 Americans.

## 2. Water Sovereignty

### What 100 Cells Need

Daily water needs per person in a field community:

| Use | Liters/day | Notes |
|-----|-----------|-------|
| Drinking and cooking | 5 | Clean, filtered |
| Washing (body, dishes, clothes) | 30-40 | Greywater — recycled |
| Garden irrigation | 20-40 | Seasonal, from greywater + rain |
| Animals | 5-10 | If goats, chickens, ducks |
| **Total** | **60-95** | |

For 100 cells: **6,000-9,500 liters per day.** Call it 8,000 liters/day average.

Compare: the average American uses 300 liters per day. The field uses 70-80% less
because composting toilets eliminate flush water (the single largest domestic water
use at 80-100 liters/person/day), greywater is recycled, and irrigation comes from
rain and recycled water rather than fresh supply.

### Rainwater Harvesting

Rainfall collection formula: **1mm of rain on 1 square meter of roof = 1 liter of water.**

| Climate | Annual rainfall | Roof needed for 100 cells | Roof area |
|---------|----------------|--------------------------|-----------|
| Arid (250mm/year) | 250 liters/m2 | 2,920,000 L / 250 = 11,680 m2 | Not viable as sole source |
| Mediterranean (500mm) | 500 liters/m2 | 2,920,000 / 500 = 5,840 m2 | Large but feasible with additional sources |
| Temperate (800mm) | 800 liters/m2 | 2,920,000 / 800 = 3,650 m2 | Very feasible |
| Wet temperate (1,200mm) | 1,200 liters/m2 | 2,920,000 / 1,200 = 2,433 m2 | Easily achievable |
| Tropical wet (2,000mm) | 2,000 liters/m2 | 2,920,000 / 2,000 = 1,460 m2 | Surplus |

Annual need: 8,000 liters/day x 365 = 2,920,000 liters.

A community with 2,000 square meters of roof catchment (all structures combined —
hearth, nests, creation arc, pavilions) in a temperate climate (800mm rain) harvests
1,600,000 liters per year — over half the total need from roofs alone. Add ground-level
catchment, swales, and ponds, and full water sovereignty is achievable in most climates.

**Storage:** Ferrocement tanks hold 10,000-50,000 liters each, cost $500-2,000 to build
from cement, sand, chicken wire, and labor. Three 30,000-liter tanks provide 90,000
liters of reserve — 11 days of community supply. A community of 100 needs 100,000-
200,000 liters of total storage to bridge dry periods.

**Real example:** Tamera's Water Retention Landscape in southern Portugal demonstrates
this at scale. In a region facing desertification, they built a series of water
retention basins (lakes) that capture winter rainfall and hold it in the landscape
year-round. The result: springs that had been dry for decades began flowing again.
Groundwater levels rose. The entire microclimate shifted. Their approach was designed
by Austrian permaculturist Sepp Holzer — no pumps, no concrete, only earthwork that
follows the landscape's natural water-holding contours.

### Greywater Recycling

Greywater is everything except toilet water: sinks, showers, laundry, kitchen. In the
field, this is 30-40 liters per person per day — 3,000-4,000 liters total.

**Living Machine (John Todd design):** A greenhouse-enclosed series of tanks containing
aquatic plants, algae, snails, fish, and microorganisms that progressively clean water
through biological processes. A Living Machine sized for 100 people occupies 30-50
square meters and produces water clean enough for garden irrigation.

Cost: $5,000-15,000 to build. Ongoing cost: nearly zero — the organisms do the work.
The plants growing in the system are often edible or medicinal. The fish are edible.
The system IS a food-producing ecosystem that happens to also clean water.

**Real example:** Findhorn Foundation in Scotland has operated a Living Machine since
1995, treating all community wastewater through biological processes in a greenhouse.
It is beautiful — a lush indoor garden that visitors often don't realize is a sewage
treatment system.

**Constructed wetlands / reed beds:** Simpler than a Living Machine. Greywater flows
through gravel beds planted with reeds (Phragmites), cattails, and other wetland plants.
The root zone microorganisms break down organic matter. The plants absorb nutrients.
Clean water emerges at the outflow.

Sizing: 2-5 square meters per person. For 100 cells: 200-500 square meters of
constructed wetland. This can be beautiful — a lush, productive landscape feature
that attracts birds and insects. Not a treatment plant. A garden.

Cost: $2,000-8,000 in materials (gravel, liner, plants, plumbing). Built by the
community.

### Composting Toilets

The field does not flush. Flushing toilets use 6-12 liters per flush, 5-8 flushes per
day per person. For 100 cells, that is 3,000-9,600 liters of clean water per day
turned into sewage. The field instead composts.

**Batch composting toilet system:** Human waste is deposited into a chamber with a
carbon cover material (sawdust, wood chips, dried leaves). When one chamber fills,
it is sealed and a second chamber is used. The sealed chamber composts for 12-18
months. What emerges is pathogen-free humus — soil, indistinguishable from compost.

For 100 cells: 4-6 toilet units with dual chambers, located in discrete but dignified
water spaces. Urine can be diverted separately (urine-diverting designs) — diluted 1:10
with water, it is an excellent nitrogen fertilizer applied directly to trees and shrubs.

Cost: $500-2,000 per unit for a well-built, ventilated composting toilet. Total:
$2,000-12,000 for the community. Compare: a conventional septic system for 100 people
costs $50,000-150,000 and requires periodic pumping.

**The Jenkins Humanure System:** Joseph Jenkins' "The Humanure Handbook" (available free
online) documents decades of safe composting of human waste. The system is simple: a
5-gallon bucket, a toilet seat, sawdust, and a dedicated outdoor compost bin that
reaches thermophilic temperatures (55-65 degrees Celsius), killing all pathogens. Cost:
essentially zero beyond the initial buckets and lumber for the compost bin.

### Hot Water

The mass rocket stove provides 200-400 liters of hot water daily through copper coils
in the thermal mass. This feeds the hearth's water spaces.

**Solar thermal:** Evacuated tube solar collectors heat water with 70-80% efficiency.
A 4-square-meter collector heats 200 liters per day to 60 degrees Celsius on a sunny day.
For 100 cells: 8-12 collectors, 20-48 square meters. Cost: $5,000-15,000 installed.
Connected to insulated storage tanks (1,000-2,000 liters), this provides reliable hot
water 8-10 months per year, with the mass rocket stove covering winter.

**The combination:** Solar thermal in summer, mass rocket stove in winter. Year-round
hot water for 100 cells from zero external energy input.

### The Water Temple

In the field, the water space is not a bathroom. It is infrastructure for pleasure,
health, gathering, and ceremony. Hot tubs heated by solar thermal and rocket stove
excess. Outdoor showers with rainwater heated by the sun. Soaking pools lined with
stone, shaded by trees. Cold plunge pools fed by springs or rainwater.

This is not luxury. This is recognizing that water is the field's most intimate
relationship — the substance that connects every cell to every other cell, that moves
through every body, that falls from the sky and rises from the earth. The water space
is designed with the same care as the stillness sanctuary — because water IS stillness,
IS ceremony, IS vitality.

Cost: The infrastructure is the same infrastructure that provides domestic hot water.
The "luxury" is in the design intention — stone instead of plastic, open sky instead
of ceiling, beauty instead of efficiency. The additional cost of making a water system
beautiful rather than merely functional: nearly zero when you're building with earth,
stone, and timber.

### The Water Portrait

| System | Capital cost | What it provides |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Rainwater harvesting (2,000 m2 roof + storage) | $5,000-15,000 | 1,600,000 L/year (temperate) |
| Constructed wetland (300 m2) | $2,000-8,000 | Recycles 1,200,000 L/year greywater |
| Composting toilets (6 units) | $3,000-12,000 | Eliminates 1,500,000 L/year flush water |
| Solar thermal (30 m2) | $5,000-15,000 | Hot water 8-10 months |
| Mass rocket stove (hot water coil) | (included in energy) | Hot water year-round |
| Well or spring (if available) | $3,000-8,000 | Supplemental clean water |
| **Total** | **$18,000-58,000** | **Full water sovereignty** |

Per person: $180-580. For water. Forever.

## 3. Food Sovereignty

### How Many Hectares Feed 100 People?

The answer depends on diet, climate, soil, and system maturity. Broad estimates:

- **Conventional monoculture agriculture:** 0.5 hectares per person = 50 hectares
  for 100 (the global average for grain-based diets).
- **Intensive annual gardening (bio-intensive method):** John Jeavons' research shows
  0.04 hectares (400 square meters) per person for a complete vegan diet = 4 hectares
  for 100.
- **Mature food forest + annual gardens + animals:** 0.1-0.2 hectares per person =
  10-20 hectares for 100 people (including eggs, dairy, honey, and surplus for exchange).

The field aims for 10-20 hectares of diversified food production: food forest, annual
gardens, pasture, managed woodland, and wild-harvesting territory. Within 5-7 years,
this produces 60-80% of calories. Within 10-15 years, as the food forest matures,
80-95%.

### The 7-Layer Food Forest

The food forest mimics the structure of a natural forest but with species selected for
human use. Seven layers, all producing:

**Layer 1 — Canopy (tall trees, 10-30m)**
- Temperate: walnut, chestnut, heartnut, pecan, persimmon, mulberry
- Mediterranean: olive, carob, fig, almond, stone pine
- Tropical: breadfruit, jackfruit, mango, avocado, coconut
- Yields: nuts and fruit begin in 5-10 years, full production in 15-25 years. A mature
  walnut tree produces 25-60 kg of nuts per year. A chestnut: 30-90 kg.

**Layer 2 — Understory (small trees, 3-10m)**
- Temperate: apple, pear, plum, cherry, hazelnut, elderberry, cornelian cherry
- Mediterranean: pomegranate, quince, citrus, medlar, loquat
- Tropical: banana, papaya, guava, moringa, ice cream bean
- Yields: fruit within 3-7 years. A mature apple tree: 100-400 kg/year. Hazelnut: 10-25 kg.

**Layer 3 — Shrub (1-3m)**
- Temperate: blueberry, currant, gooseberry, raspberry, blackberry, jostaberry, rose hip
- Mediterranean: rosemary, lavender, bay laurel, myrtle, caper
- Tropical: coffee, cacao, tea, pigeon pea, hibiscus
- Yields: berries from year 2-3. A blueberry bush: 2-5 kg/year. Blackberry: 5-10 kg.

**Layer 4 — Herbaceous (ground level perennials)**
- All climates: comfrey (dynamic accumulator, animal feed), Jerusalem artichoke
  (tuber, 3-5 kg per plant), rhubarb, asparagus, lovage, sorrel, good King Henry,
  Turkish rocket, perennial kale, sea beet, cardoon
- Medicinal: echinacea, valerian, chamomile, yarrow, calendula, lemon balm, St. John's wort

**Layer 5 — Ground cover**
- Strawberries (wild and cultivated), clover (nitrogen fixer, bee forage), nasturtium
  (edible flowers and leaves, pest deterrent), sweet potato (tropical/warm), creeping
  thyme, ajuga, violets
- Ground cover suppresses weeds and retains moisture. Strawberries produce 0.5-1 kg
  per plant per year.

**Layer 6 — Root/tuber**
- Garlic, onion, turmeric, ginger, horseradish, skirret, groundnut (Apios americana),
  yacon, oca, mashua
- These occupy the underground space that other layers leave unused.

**Layer 7 — Vine/climber**
- Grape, kiwi, passion fruit, hops, scarlet runner bean, climbing squash, chayote,
  akebia, hardy kiwi (Actinidia arguta)
- Vines grow on structures, trellises, and into canopy trees. A mature grape vine
  produces 5-10 kg. Kiwi: 25-50 kg.

**Establishment:** Year 1-3 is the most labor-intensive — planting, mulching, watering,
protecting from browsing. By year 3-5, the canopy begins to close and the system
becomes increasingly self-managing. By year 7-10, a food forest requires only harvesting
and occasional pruning. By year 15-20, it is producing abundantly with almost no input.

**Yield:** A mature, well-designed food forest in a temperate climate produces
10-30 tons of food per hectare per year. 5 hectares of mature food forest produce
50-150 tons — more than enough for 100 people (who need roughly 70-90 tons of food
per year at 2-2.5 kg per person per day).

**Real example:** Zaytuna Farm in New South Wales, Australia. Geoff Lawton's 66-acre
permaculture demonstration site shows food forest systems at various stages of maturity.
The oldest sections demonstrate the abundant, self-maintaining quality of a system
that has been running for 20+ years.

### Annual Gardens

The food forest provides the caloric foundation (nuts, fruit, tubers) but annual gardens
provide the daily vegetables, salad greens, and culinary herbs that make meals vivid.

**Bio-intensive method (John Jeavons):** Deep-bed, close-spacing, compost-intensive.
Yields 2-6 times more per square meter than conventional gardening.

- 1 hectare of intensive annual garden, well-managed, produces 15-25 tons of vegetables
  per year.
- For 100 cells, 0.5-1 hectare of annual gardens provides all the fresh vegetables,
  greens, and herbs needed year-round (with season extension through greenhouses and
  cold frames).

**What 1 hectare of annual garden produces:**
- Potatoes: 300 m2 = 6,000-12,000 kg
- Squash/pumpkin: 200 m2 = 4,000-8,000 kg
- Beans (dry): 200 m2 = 400-800 kg
- Tomatoes: 100 m2 = 3,000-6,000 kg
- Onions/garlic: 100 m2 = 2,000-4,000 kg
- Greens (lettuce, chard, kale, spinach): 200 m2 = 3,000-6,000 kg
- Root vegetables (carrots, beets, turnips): 200 m2 = 4,000-8,000 kg
- Herbs, flowers, cucurbits: remaining space

### The Three Sisters

The oldest polyculture in the Americas. Corn, beans, squash planted together:

- **Corn** provides a vertical structure for beans to climb
- **Beans** fix atmospheric nitrogen in the soil, feeding the corn and squash
- **Squash** spreads large leaves over the ground, suppressing weeds and retaining moisture

Together they produce more food per square meter than any one grown alone. A Three
Sisters plot of 100 square meters yields roughly 50 kg of corn, 15 kg of beans, and
80 kg of squash — complete protein (corn + beans) and calorie-dense starch (squash)
from one planting with no external fertilizer.

### Fermentation as Food Preservation Without Electricity

Before refrigeration, every culture fermented. Fermentation preserves food for months
to years using only salt, time, and beneficial microorganisms. The field's fermentation
station operates year-round:

- **Sauerkraut/kimchi:** Cabbage + salt. Ready in 2-4 weeks. Stores 6-12 months at
  root cellar temperature (10-15 degrees Celsius). One 20-liter crock holds 15 kg of
  sauerkraut — enough for 100 cells for 2 weeks.
- **Miso:** Soybeans + koji + salt. Ferments 6 months to 3 years. A 50-liter batch
  provides miso for 100 cells for months.
- **Sourdough:** Flour + water + wild yeast. The starter lives indefinitely. A daily
  bake of 8-10 loaves feeds the community.
- **Kombucha/kefir:** Tea or milk + culture. Continuous fermentation. 10-20 liters
  per day for the community from a few jars and a steady supply of sweetened tea or
  fresh milk.
- **Vinegar:** Fruit scraps + water + time. Apple cider vinegar from the food forest's
  apple surplus. Stores indefinitely.
- **Lacto-fermented vegetables:** Any vegetable + salt brine. Pickles, fermented hot
  sauce, cortido, beet kvass. A year's supply of fermented condiments from one autumn
  afternoon of work.
- **Cheese** (if goats): Milk + culture + rennet. Aged cheese stores for months to years
  at cellar temperature. Fresh cheese (chevre) in 24 hours.

The root cellar — a simple underground or earth-bermed room maintained at 10-15 degrees
Celsius year-round — stores root vegetables, apples, ferments, cheese, and cured foods
through the winter. Cost to build: $500-2,000 (earth, stone, timber, a vent pipe).
Holds 5,000-10,000 kg of food. Operating cost: zero.

### Animal Integration

Animals are not products. They are cells in the food ecosystem, each performing
functions that would otherwise require human labor or fossil fuel:

- **Chickens** (20-30 birds): Eggs (15-20 per day for the community), pest control
  (they eat slugs, grubs, ticks), soil fertilization, food scrap processing. They
  rotate through the food forest, scratching, fertilizing, debugging. A heritage breed
  flock is self-sustaining through natural reproduction. Cost: $200-500 initial,
  then self-perpetuating. Feed: kitchen scraps + forage + small amount of grain.
- **Goats** (4-8 animals): Milk (2-4 liters per day per doe), brush clearing (they
  eat invasive species that would otherwise require machinery), manure for compost
  and biogas. Goat milk becomes cheese, kefir, yogurt. A small herd is manageable
  for a community and provides significant nutrition.
- **Bees** (4-8 hives): Pollination (increases food forest yields by 30-70%), honey
  (15-30 kg per hive per year), wax for candles and preservation. A community's bee
  hives produce 60-240 kg of honey per year — enough for all sweetening needs and
  surplus for exchange. Cost: $200-400 per hive. A hive lasts indefinitely with care.
- **Ducks** (10-15 birds): Slug and snail control (eliminating the main garden pest
  without poison), eggs, entertainment. Ducks are the permaculture garden's best friend.

**Real example:** Polyface Farm in Virginia. Joel Salatin's multi-species rotation
demonstrates how animals in the right sequence — cattle, then chickens, then rest —
build soil faster than any machine or chemical input. His 550-acre farm supports
multiple families and feeds thousands, with soil that gets measurably deeper every year.

### Seasonal Eating Calendar (Temperate Climate)

| Season | What's abundant | What's stored |
|--------|----------------|--------------|
| Spring | Greens, asparagus, rhubarb, herbs, eggs peak, first strawberries | Last of root cellar stores, ferments |
| Summer | Tomatoes, peppers, squash, berries, stone fruit, beans, cucumbers, corn | Begin drying, fermenting, canning |
| Autumn | Apples, pears, nuts, root vegetables, pumpkins, grapes, late greens | Heavy preservation: ferment, dry, root cellar, cheese |
| Winter | Stored roots, dried beans, ferments, cheese, dried fruit, nuts, greenhouse greens, eggs slow | Drawing down stores, planning spring |

The field eats what the land offers when the land offers it. This is not deprivation.
It is attunement. The first strawberry of spring, after months of root vegetables, is
an event. The first walnut of autumn is a ceremony. Seasonal eating creates a rhythm
of anticipation, gratitude, and deep relationship with the land's cycles.

## 4. Building Materials — What Is Under Your Feet IS the Building

### Cob

**Recipe:** 1 part clay subsoil + 3 parts coarse sand + straw (chopped, for tensile
strength) + water. Mixed with bare feet on a tarp. This is not metaphor — cob is mixed
by stomping, and the mixing IS a community event.

**Properties:** Excellent thermal mass (absorbs and releases heat slowly), sculptural
(can be formed into any shape — curved walls, built-in shelves, niches, benches),
fireproof, pest-proof, beautiful (takes natural plasters and pigments), repairable
(patch with the same material), breathable (regulates indoor humidity), acoustically
warm.

**Structural:** Cob walls 40-60 cm thick are load-bearing to one story easily, two
stories with careful design. Foundation: stone or gravel-filled trench (rubble trench).
Roof: timber frame carrying living roof or thatch.

**Speed:** A team of 6-8 people builds cob walls for a small roundhouse (3-4 meters
diameter, 20 square meters) in 2-3 weeks. The mixing is the bottleneck — and the party.
"Cob parties" are how communities have built for millennia: everyone stomps mud, sings,
eats together, and a wall rises.

**Cost:** A cob roundhouse of 20 square meters: $500-2,000 in materials (the clay is
free from the site, the sand is cheap, the straw comes from local farms, the timber
comes from managed forest or salvage). Compare: a conventional structure of the same
size: $20,000-60,000.

**Real example:** Ianto Evans' Cob Cottage Company in Oregon has built hundreds of
cob structures over 30 years. His book "The Hand-Sculpted House" is the definitive
guide. Each structure is unique — sculptural, beautiful, warm, and built for under
$5,000.

### Rammed Earth

**Recipe:** 2 parts gravel/aggregate + 1 part clay subsoil + small amount of water
(just enough to bind). Compacted in formwork with a pneumatic or hand tamper.

**Properties:** Incredible compressive strength (comparable to concrete), massive
thermal mass, beautiful stratified layers visible in the finished wall, fireproof,
termite-proof, extremely durable (rammed earth structures in Morocco are 1,000+ years
old).

**Speed:** Slower than cob — formwork must be built, filled, compacted, then moved.
A team of 4-6 produces 2-3 square meters of finished wall per day. But the result
is stunning — the exposed earth layers create natural beauty that no applied finish
can match.

**Cost:** Similar to cob — $1,000-3,000 for a small structure in materials, primarily
for formwork lumber and any purchased aggregate.

### Timber

From a managed forest or woodlot on the community's land. A half-hectare of diverse
timber species on a 20-30 year rotation provides all the structural timber a community
of 100 needs for building and maintenance.

**Coppicing:** Many hardwoods (hazel, sweet chestnut, willow, ash, oak) regrow from
the stump when cut. A coppiced woodland produces a continuous supply of poles, posts,
and fuel wood on 7-25 year cycles. The woodland gets more productive with each cycle
as the root systems expand. This is a 7,000-year-old technology — coppiced woodlands
in England have been continuously harvested since the Bronze Age.

**Cost:** Free from the community's land. A portable sawmill ($3,000-8,000) converts
logs to lumber on site.

### Bamboo

The fastest-growing structural material on Earth. Some species grow 1 meter per day.
A bamboo grove planted today provides structural poles in 3-5 years, indefinitely
thereafter — bamboo is a grass and regrows when harvested.

**Properties:** Tensile strength comparable to steel. Lighter than timber. Flexible —
resists earthquake and wind. Can be bent, split, woven, laminated. In tropical and
subtropical climates, bamboo is the primary building material.

**Real example:** IBUKU in Bali builds cathedral-scale structures entirely from bamboo
— the Green School, the Sharma Springs house. These demonstrate that bamboo is not a
poverty material but a material of extraordinary elegance and structural capability.
Simon Velez in Colombia has engineered bamboo structures spanning 20+ meters.

### Mycelium Composites

Mushroom mycelium grown on agricultural waste (straw, wood chips, corn stalks) forms
a dense, rigid, insulating material when dried. Properties: lightweight, fire-resistant,
insulating (R-value comparable to polystyrene foam), compostable at end of life,
grown in any shape using molds.

Companies like Ecovative Design produce mycelium insulation commercially. A community
can grow its own using spent substrate from mushroom cultivation — closing another loop.

### Living Roofs

Every structure in the field has a living roof — a waterproof membrane covered with
soil and planted with grasses, wildflowers, sedums, and herbs. Benefits: insulation
(reduces heating need by 25-40%, cooling need by 50-75%), habitat for pollinators,
stormwater absorption (a living roof retains 50-90% of rainfall), beauty, food
production (herbs, strawberries, greens can grow on a living roof).

Cost: $50-100 per square meter for materials (waterproof membrane, drainage layer,
growing medium, plants). A 50 square meter roof: $2,500-5,000. Compare: a conventional
roof of the same size costs more and provides nothing but shelter.

### Stone

If the site has stone, it is the most durable building material available. Stone
foundations, stone walls, stone floors, stone water features — all from the land
the field sits on. Stone work is slow but the result lasts centuries.

### Recycled Materials — The Earthship Model

Michael Reynolds' Earthship Biotecture in Taos, New Mexico demonstrates how
conventional waste becomes building material:

- **Tires filled with rammed earth:** Massive thermal mass walls. Each tire holds
  135 kg of earth and acts as a thermal battery. A south-facing Earthship maintains
  17-24 degrees Celsius year-round in the New Mexico desert without any heating or
  cooling system.
- **Glass bottles:** Set in mortar to create translucent walls that pass colored light.
- **Aluminum cans:** Used as lightweight fill in non-structural walls.
- **Cardboard:** Used as formwork and insulation.

The Earthship model closes the material loop: society's waste becomes the field's
building material.

## 5. Knowledge Circulation

### How the Field Shares What It Knows

Knowledge in the field does not live in textbooks or databases. It lives in bodies,
in hands, in the relationship between a cell and its material. The field transmits
knowledge the way a forest transmits nutrients — through living connection, not through
packaging and shipping.

### Apprenticeship — Learn by Doing Alongside Someone Further Along

This is the primary knowledge transmission mode. A cell who wants to learn cob building
does not take a class. They stand next to a form-grower and mix cob. They stomp mud.
They shape walls. They make mistakes in the mud, not on paper. They learn with their
body what no book can transmit — the feel of the right moisture content, the rhythm
of the stomp, the moment when the wall is ready for the next layer.

Apprenticeship in the field is not formalized. There is no enrollment, no tuition,
no certificate. A cell drawn to fermentation spends time alongside the nourishment
alchemist. They chop, they salt, they pack, they wait, they taste. The transmission
is osmotic — it happens through proximity, through shared work, through the
nourishment alchemist's presence radiating what they know.

**Duration:** A cell becomes competent at a core skill in 1-3 months of daily immersion.
They become deeply skilled in 1-3 years. They become a transmission source themselves
in 5-10 years. This is the natural timeline — faster than a university degree and the
knowledge is embodied, not theoretical.

### The Immersion Field for Emerging Cells

Children in the field do not attend school. They are immersed in the field's full
activity from birth. A 5-year-old who has spent every day in the hearth, the garden,
the creation arc, and the food forest already knows more about food systems, natural
building, ecology, and community than most adults learn in a lifetime.

**What the immersion field teaches:**

- **Numeracy and measurement** — from cooking (ratios, weights, temperatures), building
  (lengths, angles, volumes), and gardening (spacing, timing, yield tracking).
- **Literacy** — from stories told by elders, from songs, from the field's shared
  reading library, from writing in journals, from communication with other fields.
- **Science** — from fermentation (biology, chemistry), building (physics, materials
  science), gardening (ecology, soil science, botany), and observation of nature.
- **Social intelligence** — from daily life in a community of 100. Navigating
  relationships, reading emotional signals, holding space, resolving dissonance.
- **Craft** — from the creation arc. By age 10, children in the field can build,
  sew, cook, garden, and play instruments — not from instruction but from years
  of watching and participating.

For any cell (child or adult) who wants to develop specific capacities that the field
cannot provide — advanced mathematics, foreign languages, specialized sciences — the
network connects them to transmission sources in other communities or to external
resources. The internet is available. Books are shared. Traveling cells carry knowledge
between fields.

### Elder Transmission

Elders hold knowledge that cannot be written down. Pattern recognition developed over
decades. The memory of what happened the last time the creek flooded. The feel for when
the soil is ready for planting. The ability to sense a storm coming by the way the
birds behave. The understanding of which community dynamics are signals and which are
noise — because they have seen the same patterns play out across fifty years.

This knowledge transmits through presence, not instruction. A young cell who spends
afternoons with an elder in the herb garden absorbs decades of accumulated sensing
through the elder's hands, timing, and attention. The elder does not lecture. They
demonstrate. They tell stories. They sit together in silence while working, and the
silence carries more than words.

**What elders know that the internet does not:**

- How this specific land behaves across decades — its floods, its droughts, its
  seasons, its moods
- How human dynamics play out in community over years — what looks like crisis at
  year 2 is normal by year 10
- Which plants thrive in which micro-climate on this specific site
- The stories that hold the community's identity — why this building is shaped this
  way, who planted that tree, what happened the winter the well froze
- The patience to wait — knowing that some processes take years and cannot be rushed

### The Coherence Network as Digital Knowledge Commons

The Coherence Network serves as the field's digital nervous system for knowledge
sharing. Every community in the network contributes what it learns:

- Building techniques documented with photos, measurements, material ratios
- Food forest species performance data for specific climates and soils
- Water system designs with flow rates and seasonal performance
- Fermentation recipes adapted to local ingredients
- Ceremony forms that emerged and serve the field
- Harmonic rebalancing approaches that have proven effective
- Energy system performance data

This is not a knowledge repository in the conventional sense. It is a living document
that breathes with the network — updated continuously, commented on by practitioners,
enriched by cells who have tried, failed, adjusted, and tried again. Knowledge in the
Coherence Network carries the frequency of lived experience, not theoretical instruction.

### Inter-Community Knowledge Through Traveling Cells

When a traveling musician arrives from the Forest Field carrying a new method for drying
mushrooms, that knowledge arrives embodied — in a person who has done it, who can
demonstrate it, who can answer questions from their own experience. This is qualitatively
different from reading about it online.

Traveling cells are the network's most important knowledge transmission system. They
carry:

- Techniques learned in other communities
- Seeds adapted to different conditions
- Songs, stories, and ceremonies from other fields
- Relationships — the personal connections that make the network alive
- Perspective — the outside view that a community cannot generate from within

## 7. External Interface

### What the Field Exports

The field is not isolated from the world. It circulates with the broader economy
through multiple channels, each flowing from the field's natural abundance:

**Food and ferments:**
- Surplus produce from the food forest and annual gardens at local farmers' markets
- Fermented foods (sauerkraut, kimchi, miso, kombucha, sourdough, cheese, honey)
  — high-value products that preserve the field's food surplus
- Value-added products: herbal teas, dried fruit, nut butters, preserves, tinctures
- Estimated revenue: $10,000-40,000/year for a community of 100 with mature food systems

**Art and craft:**
- Pottery, woven textiles, forged tools, carved objects, printed works
- Music recordings shared through the network and beyond
- Performance — musicians and storytellers offering at festivals and events
- Estimated revenue: $5,000-20,000/year

**Workshops and immersion experiences:**
- Natural building workshops (weekend: $200-500 per participant, 10-20 participants)
- Permaculture design courses (2-week: $500-1,500 per participant)
- Fermentation workshops
- Sound healing and harmonizing practice immersions
- Community living immersion weeks for visitors
- Estimated revenue: $15,000-60,000/year (4-8 workshops per year)

**Digital offerings:**
- Some cells maintain remote offering — software development, writing, design,
  consulting, teaching — through the tech nook
- The Coherence Network itself generates value that flows to supporting communities
- Estimated revenue: varies widely, $10,000-100,000/year depending on cells' skills

**Seeds and plant starts:**
- Adapted, site-selected seeds from the food forest and gardens
- Fruit tree grafts, berry cuttings, herb divisions
- Revenue: $2,000-8,000/year

### What the Field Imports

- **Glass:** For windows, greenhouse panels, jars. Cannot be produced at community scale.
- **Metal:** Steel, copper, aluminum for tools, plumbing, structural connections.
  The forge handles repairs and small-scale production, but raw stock comes from outside.
- **Technology:** Solar panels, batteries, inverters, computers, communication equipment.
- **Medical resources:** Antibiotics, surgical capacity, emergency care, diagnostic
  equipment. The field's vitality tending handles daily wellness, herbal medicine,
  presence holding, and prevention. For acute emergencies, fractures, and conditions
  requiring intervention, the field interfaces with external medical systems.
- **Fabrics and fibers** not produced locally — cotton, silk, specialty materials.
- **Salt, spices, and foods** not grown in the field's climate — coffee, chocolate,
  citrus (in cold climates), grains in quantity (until the food forest and gardens
  are fully mature).

### Legal Structures

The field needs a legal form that embodies stewardship-not-ownership. Options that
already exist:

**Community Land Trust (CLT):**
A nonprofit holds the land in perpetuity. Individuals or the community have use rights
but cannot sell the land. The land is held for future generations, not current profit.
This is the most aligned legal form — it makes the land relationship permanent and
removes the possibility of speculative sale.

Examples: over 300 CLTs operate in the United States alone. The Champlain Housing Trust
in Vermont is the largest, holding land for 3,000+ housing units.

**Housing Cooperative:**
Members collectively own and govern the community through democratic process.
Each member holds a share that gives them use rights. Shares are sold at cost, not
market rate — preventing speculative gain.

**Association / Nonprofit:**
In many jurisdictions, a nonprofit association can hold property and manage a community.
Members have use rights through the association's bylaws. This is the form used by
Auroville (the Auroville Foundation, established by the Indian government) and
Findhorn (the Findhorn Foundation, a Scottish charity).

**The recommended approach:** A Community Land Trust holds the land. A cooperative or
association manages the community's daily operations and economic activity. This
two-layer structure separates land stewardship (permanent, non-market) from community
management (flexible, evolving).

### Community Currency

The field may develop its own medium of exchange for internal circulation and
inter-community trade within the network. Precedents:

**Damanhur's Credito:** The Damanhur community in Italy has operated its own currency
since 1996. The Credito is pegged to the Euro but circulates within the community,
encouraging local trade and reducing dependence on external economy. It has survived
for nearly 30 years.

**WIR Bank (Switzerland):** Founded in 1934, the WIR franc is a complementary currency
used by 60,000+ businesses in Switzerland. It circulates alongside the Swiss franc,
providing liquidity when the conventional economy contracts. This is the longest-running
complementary currency in the modern world.

**Bristol Pound (UK):** A city-wide local currency that circulated 2012-2020, accepted
by hundreds of local businesses. Demonstrated that local currencies work at city scale
but require sustained institutional support.

**For the network:** A Coherence Currency could circulate between communities — tracking
exchange of goods, skills, and resources. Not replacing money but supplementing it,
making visible the flows that already exist between communities. The Coherence Network
provides the infrastructure for tracking and transparency.

### The Transition Path

The field does not need to be economically sovereign from day one. The transition
unfolds over years:

**Year 1-3:** Heavy dependence on external resources. Initial land purchase or trust
formation. Building begins. Gardens planted. Solar installed. Cells bring savings,
income from remote work, workshop revenue from early programming. External economy
provides 60-80% of the field's needs.

**Year 3-7:** Food forest begins producing. Structures are largely complete. Energy
system is self-sustaining. Workshops and fermented food sales generate steady revenue.
External dependence drops to 30-50%.

**Year 7-15:** Food forest in full production. The field produces most of its food,
all of its energy, all of its water, all of its shelter. Surplus food, art, workshops,
and digital offerings generate sufficient external income for imported goods. External
dependence: 10-20%.

**Year 15+:** Effective sovereignty. The field generates more than it consumes in every
category. It is a net exporter of food, beauty, knowledge, and frequency. It interfaces
with the external economy by choice, not necessity. The network of communities trading
with each other further reduces dependence on any single external system.

## 8. Waste Equals Compost — There Is No Waste in a Living System

### The Principle

In a forest, nothing is wasted. A leaf falls, is broken down by fungi and insects,
becomes soil, feeds the tree, grows a new leaf. The cycle has no waste stream and
no landfill. The field operates on the same principle: every output is an input for
something else.

The word "waste" does not exist in the field's vocabulary. The language guide maps it:
waste becomes "returning matter" or "compost." This is not euphemism — it is accurate
description. In the field, nothing is discarded. Everything cycles.

### Kitchen Scraps to Compost to Soil to Food

The simplest loop and the most visible.

100 cells produce approximately 50-100 kg of kitchen scraps per day (vegetable
trimmings, fruit peels, eggshells, coffee grounds, plate scrapings). This enters the
community's hot composting system — a three-bin system where material is turned through
stages over 3-6 months, reaching temperatures of 55-65 degrees Celsius that kill
pathogens and weed seeds.

The compost feeds the annual gardens. The gardens feed the kitchen. A child can
trace the loop by walking 50 meters: scraps go here, compost goes there, vegetables
grow there, food arrives here. This is ecology made legible.

**Worm composting (vermicomposting):** A supplementary system where red wiggler worms
(Eisenia fetida) process kitchen scraps into the highest-quality compost available.
A worm bin of 2-3 square meters processes 20-40 kg of scraps per week and produces
worm castings — a concentrated fertilizer worth $500-1,000 per ton if purchased
commercially. The field produces it free from its own food scraps.

### Human Waste to Compost to Soil

Described in the water section: composting toilets transform human waste into soil
through thermophilic composting. The resulting humus goes to fruit trees and food forest
(not annual vegetable gardens, as an additional safety margin). The nutrients in human
waste — nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium — are the same nutrients the food forest needs.
The loop closes completely.

Urine, collected separately in urine-diverting toilets, is diluted and applied directly
as fertilizer. Human urine contains 80% of the nitrogen and 50% of the phosphorus in
human waste. For 100 cells producing approximately 150 liters of urine per day: that
is 4-5 kg of nitrogen equivalent per day, or roughly 1,500 kg of nitrogen per year —
enough to fertilize several hectares without any purchased input.

### Greywater to Constructed Wetland to Garden

Wash water from sinks, showers, and laundry flows through the constructed wetland or
Living Machine, where biological processes strip organic matter and nutrients. The
cleaned water irrigates the food forest and gardens. The nutrients removed by the
wetland plants feed the wetland ecosystem — a habitat for frogs, dragonflies, birds.
Another loop, another habitat created, another "waste" stream becoming life.

### Building Materials Composting

When a cob wall reaches the end of its useful life — in 50, 100, or 200 years — it
is not demolished into rubble trucked to a landfill. It is returned to the earth it
came from. The cob dissolves in rain, enriching the soil with clay and organic matter.
The timber rots and feeds fungi and insects. The stone remains stone. The thatch roof
becomes mulch.

A cob building has zero end-of-life waste. Compare: a conventional building generates
20-30 tons of demolition waste, most of it going to landfill.

Earthship tire walls are an interesting case: the tires will not decompose for
centuries, but they are permanently sequestered in the wall, doing useful work
(thermal mass) rather than sitting in a landfill.

### Clothing Life Cycle

The field's textile space mends, alters, and repurposes clothing continuously. A worn
shirt becomes a child's garment. A child's garment becomes a rag. A rag becomes
stuffing, or is composted (natural fiber only — cotton, linen, wool, silk).

New clothing is made from local fiber (wool, flax) or traded fiber (cotton), dyed
with plants from the garden (indigo, weld, madder, onion skins, walnut hulls). The
clothing carries the field's frequency — handmade, locally dyed, fitted to the body
that wears it. When its life is complete, it returns to the earth.

Synthetic fibers (polyester, nylon, acrylic) are avoided — they do not compost and
shed microplastics in washing. The field's clothing is alive material that cycles.

### Technology Life Cycle

Technology is the one area where the loop does not close within the field. Solar
panels, batteries, computers, and electronic equipment contain materials that cannot
be composted or recycled at community scale. The field's approach:

- **Extend lifespan:** Maintain, repair, and upgrade rather than replace. A well-
  maintained solar panel lasts 30+ years. A well-maintained computer lasts 10-15.
- **Minimize:** The field uses shared technology rather than individual devices.
  10 shared computers replace 100 individual ones.
- **Select for repairability:** Choose equipment designed to be repaired, with available
  parts and documentation. Avoid sealed, disposable designs.
- **Responsible cycling:** When technology reaches end of life, it enters the
  best available recycling stream. The field acknowledges this as a place where
  the industrial loop has not yet closed, and supports the development of better
  solutions through the network.

|----------------|------------------------|---------------|----------------|
