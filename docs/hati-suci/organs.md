## ⚡ Electricity

*The nervous current. Right now we know the price of a kWh precisely and
the Hub's actual draw not at all — that asymmetry is the first thing to close.*

**Now.** Grid-tied to **PLN** (the national utility — tariffs are
identical across Indonesia; only the small street-lighting tax varies by
regency). Connection size per house, in **VA**, sets both the price band
and the ceiling on simultaneous load — *to sense per dwelling.* At ~600 m
the climate is ~5 °C cooler than the coast, so passive cooling and fans
can carry much of the year; AC is the dominant swing factor where present.

**Cost.** PLN residential tariff (Oct 2025, **frozen through June 2026**):

| Connection | Rp / kWh | ≈ USD / kWh |
|---|---|---|
| 900 VA (non-subsidised) | 1,352 | $0.075 |
| 1,300 · 2,200 VA | 1,444.70 | $0.080 |
| 3,500 – 5,500 VA | 1,699.53 | $0.094 |

Order-of-magnitude for the whole property: at ~1,500–3,000 kWh/month
across 6 houses + pool pump + shared spaces, ~Rp 2.2M–4.3M ≈
**$120–240 / month** — *a bracket to replace with three real PLN bills.*

**Stability (per dwelling).** PLN in Bali is *generally* reliable, but
the rural ring north of Ubud sees more outages and voltage dips than the
south, and the pool pump + well pump are sensitive to brownouts. Each

**Options → solar.** Installed cost in Bali ≈ **$650–1,300 / kWp**
(Rp 10–20M). A 5 kWp array ≈ $3,250–6,500 in panels + install; night and
backup need batteries (~$300–600 per usable kWh — a 10 kWh bank ≈
$3,000–6,000). Two honest paths:

- **Hybrid (grid + battery backup)** — installer-recommended for most
  Bali sites; solar covers daytime load, battery rides out outages, PLN
  stays as the safety net. Lower cost, less sovereignty.
- **Off-grid (cut the PLN cord)** — full sovereignty, the Hub's stated
  direction, paired with expanded rainwater. Needs a battery bank sized
  for the worst cloudy stretch; higher cost, complete independence.

Sizing rule for 2026: **net metering was abolished (MEMR 2/2024, May
2024)** — surplus exported to PLN earns *nothing*. Size to daytime
self-consumption, or store it. This makes batteries (or daytime-shifted
loads: pool pump, pumping, laundry at noon) the real lever.

**Tender.** *To name* — who reads the meters / pays PLN today; who would
own and maintain a solar system.

**To sense.**
1. Per-house PLN **VA** + last **3 months kWh and Rp** (off the bills/app).
2. One-month **outage log** per dwelling.
3. **Rooftop area + orientation/shading** per house (solar siting).
4. **Daytime vs night load split** (what could a daytime array actually offset).

## 💧 Water

*The blood. The good news is already in the body: the sun heats it, the
rain fills it, the worms clean it. What's unmeasured is the source mix
and whether the drinking water is safe.*

**Now.** Three sovereign systems already in place: **passive solar water
heating** (sun-warmed, near-zero running cost), **rainwater catchment**,
and **wormery (biological, non-chemical) greywater treatment** feeding
chemical-free gardens. The primary **source mix — well / subak spring /
PDAM — is *to sense.*** Tegallalang sits in UNESCO **subak** country,
where spring water moves through tiered channels and the land holds water
year-round; that is a major asset for both water and food.

**Cost of heating.** Passive solar = essentially **$0/month running**
(the sovereign choice, already installed). Alternatives, only if hot-water
demand outgrows the collectors: add solar-thermal capacity (passive
system $1,000–3,700) or a heat-pump heater (cheaper to buy, but it *spends
electricity* — couples the water organ to the power bill). Keep the sun
as the primary heater.

**Stability.** Two real fragilities to test:
- **Dry season (≈ Apr–Oct).** Rain is abundant Nov–Mar (2,200–2,700 mm/yr,
  70 %+ in the wet season) but thin in the dry months — rainwater storage
  must bridge the gap, or subak/well must carry it. *Measure tank capacity
  against dry-month demand.*
- **Groundwater quality.** Wells (typically 20–70 m) can be contaminated by
  nearby septic; "looks clean" is not "is clean." *Test before trusting it
  for drinking.*

**Options.** Expand rainwater storage for dry-season autonomy; confirm/secure
**subak** access (year-round spring flow); add **RO + UV** for certified
drinking water; close the loop further with greywater reuse (already
started via the wormery) and compost toilets (as the cacao sister land does).

**Purification & drainage (the path the water takes).**

| Stage | Present system | To confirm |
|---|---|---|
| Drinking | — | RO / UV present? where? |
| Greywater (sinks/showers) | wormery → gardens | flow rate, capacity |
| Blackwater (toilets) | — | septic / biofil / compost toilet? |
| Source | rainwater + ? | well depth, subak tap, PDAM line |
| Heating | passive solar | collectors per house |

**Tender.** *To name* — who maintains the wormery, the catchment, the pump.

**To sense.**
1. **Source mix** + well depth + whether subak is tapped.
2. **Rainwater tank capacity** vs estimated dry-season daily demand.
3. **Water tests:** drinking point(s) and greywater outflow.
4. **Map the plumbing & drainage** — supply runs, gravity vs pumped, where
   greywater and blackwater each go.

## 🌱 Food

*The tissue. The land is built to feed: volcanic soil, year-round subak
water, highland-tropical climate, chemical-free beds already in. The two
open questions are exactly the ones named — what to grow, and who tends.*

**Now.** Chemical-free gardens already established; the sister land
([cacao-hati-suci](../presences/cacao-hati-suci.md)) runs vegetable
gardens and food-forest beginnings. ~600 m, 20.8–29.9 °C year-round,
2,200–2,700 mm rain, volcanic soil, subak irrigation — a near-ideal
growing envelope, and coffee/rice country already.

**What can we grow (highland-tropical food-forest palette).**

| Layer | Crops that thrive here |
|---|---|
| Canopy / perennial | coconut, jackfruit, durian, avocado, mango (warmer spots), breadfruit, clove |
| Sub-canopy / fruit | banana, papaya, citrus (lime/orange), soursop, rambutan, **coffee** (local specialty), **cacao** (sister-land stream), vanilla |
| Shrub / greens | chili, eggplant, tomato, long beans, okra, **moringa/kelor**, pak choi, kale, lettuce |
| Ground / vine | **kangkung** (water spinach), pumpkin, cucumber, sweet potato |
| Root / spice | cassava (singkong), taro (talas), **ginger, turmeric, galangal, lemongrass** |
| Grain / paddy | rice (subak terraces) |
| Support / N-fixer | gliricidia, lamtoro, pigeon pea (chop-and-drop fertility) |

The frame is [`lc-farm-as-organism`](../vision-kb/concepts/lc-farm-as-organism.md):
closed-loop, soil-as-body, wormery compost feeding beds, polyculture as
organ rather than ornament. *Design the planting as layered food-forest
guilds, not rows — to sense which microclimates (sun/shade/wet/dry) each
bed actually has.*

**Who is tending it.** *To name* — the gardener(s) and which residents
hold the food organ, and their hours. Real tending-resources already in
the body's reach:
- [Dr. Ida Bagus Kesnawa](../presences/ida-bagus-kesnawa.md) + the
  **Asosiasi Biodinamik Indonesia** (biodynamic community, based right here
  in the Tegalalang area) — *food as medicine*, biodynamic preparations.
- [Agada Holistic](../presences/agada-holistic.md) — the 11+ member
  Tegalalang household; plant-medicine neighbors.
- The Hub's own stated intent: permaculture training + a commercial
  kitchen turning harvest into nourishment and income.

**To sense.**
1. **Map current beds** — what's planted now, bed sizes, what's producing.
2. **Sun/shade + water zones** across the 1,800 m² (the food-forest design layer).
3. **Soil test** (volcanic-fertile, but confirm pH + any depletion).
4. **Name the tender(s)** + hours/week + skill gaps a training would fill.
5. **Harvest log** — start recording what comes out of the garden weekly.
