# Sensing -- Practical Guide

> See [concept story](../concepts/lc-sensing.md) for the living frequency behind this work.

## The Morning Sensing Round

Five minutes. Every morning. The heartbeat of the day.

**Setup.** Circle on the ground, close enough to feel each other's warmth. No chairs -- the body stays present when it holds itself. No facilitator needed once the practice is established, though a rotating opener can help in the first months.

**The practice.** Each person offers one sentence -- not a plan, not a performance, not what they think they should feel. Just what is actually alive. "My chest is tight." "I dreamed about water." "Something in me wants to run." No response. No fixing. The sentences drop into the circle like stones into a still pond. Everyone feels the ripples.

**What not to do.** Do not turn the round into a check-in about logistics. Do not respond to what someone shares. Do not offer advice or encouragement. The discipline is in the receiving -- letting each person's truth land without the urge to do anything about it. Over weeks, this restraint builds the deepest trust the community will know.

## Body-Based Awareness Practices

These practices teach the individual body to become a clearer sensing instrument.

**Feet on earth.** Thirty seconds, barefoot on soil or grass, before the morning round. Feel the temperature, the texture, the pull of gravity. Let the body arrive before the mind starts narrating. This is calibration -- the way a musician tunes before playing.

**Belly and chest scan.** One hand on the belly, one on the chest. What is the body carrying? Not a thought about what might be wrong -- a sensation. Tightness, warmth, buzzing, heaviness, expansion. Name it in one word. This word is what the sensing round draws from.

**Walking awareness.** Once a week, a slow walk through the community -- fifteen minutes, no destination. Sensing the places: where does the land feel alive? Where does it feel held? Where does it feel neglected? The body registers what the eyes miss. A place that needs tending often announces itself as a feeling in the gut before the mind notices the weeds.

## The Field Reader

One person each week carries extra attention for the collective.

**The role.** Not a leader, not a therapist. An antenna. The field reader walks through the community with wider awareness -- noticing posture, energy, the quality of silence in rooms, what the children are doing, where people are gathering or avoiding. They do not intervene. They notice.

**Evening naming.** In the evening circle, the field reader speaks what they sensed beneath the surface: "There is a grief moving through us this week that no one has named." "The energy since Wednesday's meeting feels unfinished." "The garden crew is carrying something heavy." The naming is not accusation. It is the field feeling itself through one person's voice, the way a body feels a bruise through the nerve closest to it.

**Rotation.** Everyone takes a turn. The practice teaches every member to sense the field, not just their own state. Over a year, the whole community develops field-reading as a shared capacity rather than a specialized role.

## Group Sensing Practices

**The resonance check.** Before a decision, the circle pauses. Each person places a hand on their belly and notices: does this feel like expansion or contraction? Not a vote -- a reading. The facilitator names what the room is holding: "Most bellies are open. Two are tight. Let us hear what the tightness knows." This practice catches what discussion misses -- the body's intelligence about what is right and what is not yet right.

**Sensing the absent.** When someone is missing from the circle, the field reader names them: "Who is not here today, and what do we sense about that absence?" Sometimes the answer is simple -- someone is traveling. Sometimes the answer is that the community has been carrying an unspoken concern about someone who has withdrawn, and the naming is what allows the tending to begin.

**Crisis sensing.** When the field is disrupted -- after a conflict, a loss, an external shock -- the normal sensing round is not enough. A longer sit: twenty minutes of silence with the whole community, no speaking, just the shared practice of feeling what is here. The field processes collectively what no individual can hold alone. After the silence, the field reader speaks. Then whoever needs to speak, speaks. The crisis sensing is not resolution. It is the field's immune response -- the gathering of attention around whatever is wounded.

## Scaling Notes

| Community size | Sensing structure |
|---|---|
| Up to 30 | One morning round, one field reader, whole-community sensing |
| 30-80 | Cluster rounds of 8-12 plus one weekly whole-community round |
| 80-150 | Cluster rounds plus 2-3 field readers who meet weekly to share what they sense |
| 150+ | Sub-field rounds, field reader council, and seasonal whole-community sensing days |

The key at every scale: the field must be able to feel itself. When sensing breaks down -- when parts of the community stop being felt by the whole -- the organism loses coherence. The field readers are the connective tissue that keeps the sensing alive across the membrane of size.
