# Transmission Recipe Atlas -- Practical Guide

> See [concept story](../concepts/lc-transmission-recipe-atlas.md) for
> the living frequency behind this practice.

## The Practice

A recipe walk turns one source into one grounded payload. The source can
be technical, artistic, spiritual, embodied, relational, or operational.
The walk stays honest by naming what was observed, where the observer
stood, what pattern was extracted, where it was transposed, and what
proof belongs in the destination domain.

Use the walk when a source feels alive but its use is not obvious yet:
a song that keeps returning, a failure that taught more than the fix, a
teaching that wants embodiment, a video whose camera language carries as
much as the words, a spec whose shape belongs in daily practice.

## The Card

```text
source:
observed:
observer lens:
recipe:
transposed into:
payload:
proof mode:
claim boundary:
next embodiment:
```

The card is complete when a different person can try the payload without
needing the original conversation.

## How To Walk A Source

**1. Choose a live source.** Pick something with charge. Mildly
interesting sources create thin cards. Alive sources keep revealing
structure.

**2. Record only what is there.** Before interpretation, write the
observable sequence: words, pauses, camera cuts, error trace, body
state, room behavior, repeated phrase, rhythm, decision path.

**3. Sign the lens.** Name the seat of perception: engineer, healer,
dancer, product designer, facilitator, community steward, student,
strategist, witness. The lens is not a problem; unnamed lens is the
problem.

**4. Extract the recipe.** Write the state-change pattern as verbs.
Good recipes move: arrive, entrain, intensify, rupture, witness, soften,
repair, integrate, return, compost.

**5. Choose a destination.** Move the shape into a domain where it can
matter this week. The destination should have a real user, group, team,
practice, product, or decision.

**6. Build the payload.** Make the recipe actionable: a facilitation
script, checklist, onboarding flow, workshop score, body practice,
incident ritual, metric canvas, governance question set.

**7. Choose proof.** Proof belongs to the destination. A software
payload wants tests or behavior. A team ritual wants reduced repeat
rupture and cleaner repair. A teaching wants retention, reuse, and
changed action. A body practice wants state before/after and completed
movement.

**8. Name the boundary.** Say what the card is not claiming. This is
what lets playful transposition stay clean.

## Worked Example 1 -- The Repair Wake

```text
source: a deploy failed because a hidden dependency was trusted without a witness
observed: alert fatigue, unclear owner, fast patch, repeated surprise, quiet shame afterward
observer lens: reliability engineer + grief steward
recipe: rupture -> witness -> name what died -> repair smallest contract -> change one future habit
transposed into: engineering incident review
payload: The Repair Wake
proof mode: prevention task completed, repeat incident absent for 30 days, blame language decreases, owner is explicit
claim boundary: organizational repair practice; not therapy or clinical trauma work
next embodiment: run after the next near-miss, not only after a severe outage
```

Run a 35-minute incident room:

```text
0-5 min: timeline only
5-10 min: what the system showed
10-15 min: what assumption died
15-25 min: smallest broken contract and owner
25-30 min: one prevention task
30-35 min: seven-day check-in owner
```

The dead assumption is the center of the ritual. It might be "the
dashboard is enough," "the on-call knows this path," "this manual step
is harmless," or "we can ship without a witness because the change is
small." Naming it turns the incident from embarrassment into learning.

The wake has worked when the team can point to one changed behavior and
one repaired contract. The emotional tone matters, but the proof is not
the feeling alone; the proof is the behavior that no longer repeats the
same rupture.

## Worked Example 2 -- Onboarding As Ceremony

```text
source: an ecstatic dance track with arrival, pulse, invitation, build, release, and return
observed: the body understands the arc before it explains the arc
observer lens: product designer + dancer
recipe: arrive -> entrain -> choose -> intensify -> release -> integrate
transposed into: first-run product onboarding
payload: a seven-minute Coherence entry flow
proof mode: first recipe created, user can explain the system, user returns to the artifact
claim boundary: design analogy; no measured entrainment claim
next embodiment: prototype the flow on one page before making it a product surface
```

The onboarding flow:

```text
Arrive: What are you tending?
Pulse: choose one source that feels alive
Invitation: name the lens you are reading from
Build: extract the state-change verbs
Release: generate one recipe card
Silence: pause before choosing action
Return: commit one next embodiment
```

This replaces a documentation wall with a state change. A first-time
visitor should leave holding one recipe they made, not an abstract
description of what Coherence Network might do.

The flow has worked when a new person can say, in their own words,
"Coherence helps me find the recipe inside a source and make it useful
somewhere real," and can show the first card they made.

## Worked Example 3 -- Metric Spellbreaker

```text
source: measurement/observer teachings held as strategic metaphor
observed: what gets measured becomes visible; what is not measured becomes easier to sacrifice
observer lens: strategy steward + claim-boundary keeper
recipe: possibility field -> metric choice -> behavior collapse -> hidden cost -> counter-metric
transposed into: KPI design
payload: Metric Spellbreaker canvas
proof mode: fewer metric-induced harms, clearer tradeoffs, decision review catches distortions
claim boundary: quantum language is metaphor here, not physics proof
next embodiment: run before adopting any growth, reach, impact, or productivity metric
```

Before a team adopts a metric, fill this out:

```text
primary metric:
what it makes visible:
what it hides:
what behavior it rewards:
what behavior it punishes:
who pays if this metric becomes culture:
counter-metric:
review date:
kill condition:
```

Example: a community project wants to measure "number of people served."
That makes reach visible. It can hide depth, consent, relationship, and
the cost carried by volunteers. A counter-metric such as "relationships
maintained after 30 days" keeps another future available.

The canvas has worked when a metric review catches at least one
distortion before it becomes policy, or when the team changes a metric
because the hidden cost is no longer acceptable.

## Quality Check

A strong recipe card has:

- an observable source trace
- a named observer lens
- verbs instead of vague nouns
- a destination with real stakes
- a payload someone can run
- a proof mode native to that destination
- a claim boundary that prevents overreach
- a next embodiment small enough to do

A weak card usually has one of these problems:

- it summarizes the source instead of extracting movement
- it hides the observer lens
- it makes a metaphor do the work of proof
- it claims scientific authority from poetic resonance
- it stops at an idea without a payload
- it asks the next person to invent the usable form

## First Group Practice

Run this with three people in forty-five minutes.

```text
5 min: each person brings one live source
10 min: choose one source and record observations only
10 min: extract the verbs and choose a destination
10 min: build the payload card
5 min: name proof mode and claim boundary
5 min: choose the next embodiment owner
```

The room is complete when one card leaves with an owner and a date. The
other two sources can rest. The atlas grows through used cards, not
through inventories of possible cards.
