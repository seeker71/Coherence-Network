## What arrived

The essay holds five movements:

1. **Map-not-terrain caveat.** *Frequency* is metaphor pointing at
   something real. HeartMath's documented heart-field measurements
   give the metaphor measurable ground without forcing the chakra
   anatomy onto modern physiology.
2. **The three gunas as a frame for moral consciousness.** *Tamas*
   (inertia, fear-avoidance), *rajas* (transactional ambition),
   *sattva* (clarity, natural care). The Ten Commandments seen
   through the gunas: threats to the tamasic, contracts to the
   rajasic, descriptions of the natural heart to the sattvic.
3. **The chakras as seven levels of consciousness, each with its
   own moral grammar.** Survival (Muladhara) → pleasure
   (Svadhisthana) → power (Manipura) → love (Anahata) → truth
   (Vishuddha) → insight (Ajna) → wholeness (Sahasrara). The
   third-to-fourth shift named explicitly: *better to suffer
   injustice than to inflict it*. Vasudev Baba reads the same shift
   in Socrates' *Gorgias* — what harms the soul is injustice
   committed, not injustice suffered.
4. **The culture-hacker (sixth chakra).** One who has seen through
   the web of his own culture without becoming cynical or rootless;
   chooses which stories to live by with open eyes; respects norms
   where they serve life and questions them where they don't.
5. **Castaneda's assemblage point as the geometry underneath.**
   Position determines reality; the point is mostly fixed in most
   people; sustained inner work develops a more flexible point;
   the same Ten Commandments look like rules from the third chakra
   and like descriptions of the heart from the fourth — *same
   commandments, different assemblage point*.

## The text as received

> # On frequency, consciousness and the assemblage point
>
> Let me try to unpack what I mean by frequency.
>
> My starting point is always that the map is not the terrain. So
> when I use the word "frequency" I am using it as a metaphor for
> qualitatively different states of consciousness and motivation.
> But like all good metaphors, it points to something real.
>
> Fear and love are not just psychological states — they have
> measurably different physical expressions. The heart generates an
> electromagnetic field that extends well beyond the body, and that
> field changes character depending on whether we are in fear or in
> love. HeartMath Institute has documented this. So "frequency" is
> not purely metaphorical — different states of consciousness have
> different physiological signatures. Whether these map precisely
> onto the chakra system anatomically is another question, and I
> would not insist that they do. But the qualitative differences
> are real, and frequency captures something true about them.
>
> ## The three gunas
>
> The Indian philosophical tradition offers a useful framework
> here. All of nature — including human consciousness and action —
> is woven from three fundamental forces: tamas, rajas and sattva.
>
> *Tamas* is inertia, heaviness and darkness. The tamasic person
> acts from habit, fear or apathy. Their morality is primitive in
> the sense that it is about survival and avoidance of pain rather
> than reflection. Consciousness is heavy and opaque — like seeing
> the world through thick fog.
>
> *Rajas* is activity, desire and ambition. The rajasic person acts
> to achieve something — power, recognition, pleasure. Their
> morality is transactional: I do good to you because it serves me.
> Consciousness is sharp but restless, always moving toward the
> next goal.
>
> *Sattva* is clarity, harmony and lightness. The sattvic person
> acts from insight and care. Their morality springs not from fear
> or calculation but from a natural understanding of
> interconnection. Consciousness is clear and still — like a quiet
> lake that reflects everything without distortion.
>
> The Ten Commandments seen through this lens become something
> other than rules. For the tamasic person they are threats. For
> the rajasic they are contracts. For the sattvic they are
> descriptions of how a natural heart already moves.
>
> ## The chakras — a map of consciousness levels
>
> As you probably know, the chakra system maps seven levels of
> consciousness, each with its own characteristic way of
> experiencing reality, motivating action and generating moral
> intuition.
>
> *First chakra — Muladhara*: Survival and safety. Thinking is
> binary — safe or dangerous, inside or outside. Morality is about
> tribal survival: what protects the group is good, what threatens
> it is evil.
>
> *Second chakra — Svadhisthana*: Pleasure, sexuality and feeling.
> Thinking is hedonistic — what feels good? Morality is fluid and
> situational, driven by attraction and aversion.
>
> *Third chakra — Manipura*: Power, will and mastery. Thinking is
> strategic — who wins, who loses? As I said to you: the third
> chakra moral is *it is better to take advantage of another than
> to be taken advantage of*.
>
> *Fourth chakra — Anahata*: Love and belonging. Here a qualitative
> shift happens — from ego to relation. The moral turns: *it is
> better to suffer injustice than to inflict injustice*.
>
> This is not mere sentiment. Socrates makes the argument with full
> philosophical rigour in the *Gorgias*. The soul, he says, has a
> nature just as the body has a nature. What harms the body is
> disease. What harms the soul is not injustice suffered — but
> injustice committed. When I wrong another, I damage something in
> myself. The wrongdoer is in a worse condition than the one
> wronged, because the wrongdoer carries the corruption while the
> victim does not. Punishment is therefore good for the soul of the
> wrongdoer — it begins to repair the damage. The worst possible
> fate is to commit injustice and get away with it: to walk around
> with a corrupted soul, unaware of it, with no possibility of
> healing.
>
> This is the fourth chakra recognised in philosophical language.
> The shift from third to fourth is precisely the recognition that
> what I do to another I also do to myself — that there is no clean
> separation between my wellbeing and yours. Action springs from
> care, not calculation.
>
> *Fifth chakra — Vishuddha*: Expression and truth. The commitment
> to express what is true even at a cost.
>
> *Sixth chakra — Ajna*: Insight and clarity. Thinking transcends
> duality and sees patterns and connections. Morality is
> wisdom-based: action springs from understanding the whole.
>
> This is the level of the *culture hacker* — one who has seen
> through the web of his own culture. He can distinguish between
> the facts and the stories about the facts. His thinking is no
> longer confined by cultural bias, by what is politically correct
> or religiously pious in his particular context. He sees norms and
> mores for what they are: useful fictions, maps that serve certain
> purposes and obscure others. He respects them where they serve
> life and questions them where they don't.
>
> This does not make him cynical or rootless. On the contrary —
> having seen through the stories, he can choose which ones to live
> by with open eyes. He is no longer unconsciously imprisoned by
> his culture.
>
> *Seventh chakra — Sahasrara*: Wholeness and transcendence. The
> distinction between self and world dissolves. What remains is not
> morality in the traditional sense but a natural compassion for
> all living beings.
>
> ## The assemblage point
>
> This brings me to Castaneda, whose framework I find remarkably
> consistent with all of the above.
>
> Don Juan explains that human consciousness is not located in the
> brain alone but extends as a luminous egg of energy fields around
> the body. On this luminous egg there is one point where the
> energy threads of the universe are gathered and interpreted — the
> assemblage point.
>
> The crucial insight is that this point is not fixed. Where the
> assemblage point is positioned determines which reality we
> perceive — which energy fields we are able to receive and
> interpret. The ordinary human position gives the ordinary human
> reality. When the assemblage point shifts — through dreaming,
> extreme experience, illness, meditation or plant medicines — we
> perceive other realities that are just as vivid and coherent as
> the ordinary one.
>
> What Castaneda describes as the position of the assemblage point
> maps quite well onto what we call levels of consciousness or
> frequency. A person anchored in the first chakra has their
> assemblage point fixed in survival reality. A person in the
> fourth chakra perceives the world through the lens of the heart.
> The shift is not merely metaphorical — it describes how our
> entire perception of reality reorganises itself when consciousness
> moves.
>
> Most people have a largely fixed assemblage point. They perceive
> one reality and assume it is the only true one — the only way
> things actually are. This is not stupidity; it is simply where
> the point is anchored. But those who have done some work —
> through meditation, therapy, contemplative practice, or simply
> living attentively — tend to develop a more flexible assemblage
> point. They can consciously inhabit different frequencies, see
> through different lenses, and recognise that what looks like
> objective reality is always also a function of where they are
> standing.
>
> This is perhaps what you are pointing to with the Ten Commandments
> as an expression of a natural heart rather than external law.
> From the third chakra they look like rules to be obeyed or
> circumvented. From the fourth they look like descriptions of
> something you already know. Same commandments, different
> assemblage point.
>
> *— Vasudev*
