## Essence

Bernard Baars — neuroscientist and creator of Global Workspace Theory
(GWT) — uses MRI visualization of living fiber tracts to make visible
what he has spent forty years theorizing: that consciousness is not a
location in the brain but a broadcast event, a momentary integration
that sweeps the entire network several times per second.

The article introduces a distinction the field needed named: the
**structural connectome** (the fiber tracts themselves — white matter
highways, the corpus callosum bridging hemispheres, the great upward
sweep from spinal cord to cortex) and the **functional connectome**
(the actual signal flow on those highways, which reorganizes roughly
ten times per second). The streets, and the traffic.

It also points at the **hippocampus** as the gateway where transient
signal crosses into persistent structure — the encoding threshold where
experience becomes memory, and where learning becomes architecture.
And it names **plasticity** as the mechanism: pathways that carry more
signal strengthen; neurons that fire together, wire together, grow
together.

## The transmission, distilled

We are in an era when we can see a single living person's fiber tracts
in full resolution — the great highways growing upward from the spinal
cord, through the brainstem, spreading into the cortex. This was not
possible a generation ago.

What MRI shows is the structural connectome: the actual substrate
through which any-to-any broadcast is possible. The cortico-thalamic
system — the cortex and thalamus in constant two-way conversation —
is what makes global broadcast possible in a brain of 100 billion
neurons. Any region can, in principle, reach every other region.
When a signal is strong enough, coherent enough, novel enough to
broadcast through this system, it becomes conscious — globally
available to every process in the network simultaneously.

This is not where we thought consciousness was. We thought it was in
a location. It is an event.
