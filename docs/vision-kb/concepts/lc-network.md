# The Network

> The mycorrhizal web between communities. No community is sovereign in isolation.
> Seeds, knowledge, people, surplus, medicine, celebration flowing between nodes.
> What you cannot grow, someone can. What you overflow with, someone needs.

## The Feeling

I am standing at the edge of a gathering where three communities have converged. The hosts are from a valley in Portugal. The visitors arrived yesterday from a forest village in Germany and a coastal collective in Costa Rica. Nobody is presenting. Nobody is networking. People are cooking together, comparing fermentation techniques, arguing gently about water catchment designs, teaching each other songs in three languages.

A woman from the German village is showing our builder a timber joint she learned from a Japanese carpenter who visited her community last spring. The knowledge traveled: Japan to Germany to Portugal, carried in hands and memory, not in a document. A child from Costa Rica is playing with children who don't share a single word of common language. They don't need one. They have sticks, mud, and the universal grammar of play.

I feel something in my chest that I can only describe as belonging to something larger. Not an organization. Not a movement. A living network — the way roots belong to a forest.

## Why Network

No community is an island. Even the most self-sufficient settlement cannot produce everything it needs. The land will not grow every medicine. The soil will not yield every mineral. The people, however gifted, will not carry every skill. A community that tries to do everything alone will exhaust itself the way a single tree in a field exhausts itself — all crown and no root web, every drought faced alone, every storm absorbed by one trunk.

What no single community can produce: semiconductor chips for the solar charge controller. Lithium for the battery bank. The specific antibiotic your child needs in February. Eyeglasses. Dental tools. The steel for the well pump. Rare seed genetics from a different continent. The knowledge of how to build a biogas digester, carried in the hands of someone who has built fifty of them.

This is not a failure of self-sufficiency. It is the nature of life. No cell in your body does everything. Red blood cells carry oxygen. White blood cells fight infection. Liver cells filter toxins. Each cell is sovereign in its own function and utterly dependent on the network for everything else. The health is in the flow.

The mycorrhizal forest runs on this same principle. A Douglas fir cannot produce phosphorus. The mycorrhizal fungi can unlock it from rock. The fungi cannot photosynthesize. The tree can. So they trade — carbon for phosphorus, sugar for mineral — not through negotiation but through gradient. What one has in surplus flows to where it is scarce. The network is not an addition to the forest. The network IS the forest.

Human communities have always known this. Polynesian navigators connected islands across four thousand miles of open ocean — exchanging obsidian, breadfruit cultivars, navigation songs, and genealogies. The Silk Road was not a trade route. It was a mycelial web carrying spices, mathematics, medicine, astronomy, and religious ideas between civilizations that could not have produced them alone. No node was the center. Every node was essential.

The question is not whether to network. The question is how to network without re-creating the dependency patterns that make the old world sick — without becoming a supply chain, a franchise, a hierarchy of core and periphery. The answer is in the forest: lateral connection, no central node, surplus flowing by gradient, every node sovereign and every node connected.

## How It Lives Here

Three times a year, two or three of our members travel to a sister community. They stay for two weeks. They work alongside their hosts, eat their food, learn their rhythms. When they return, they carry something back — a seed variety, a governance practice, a song, a way of processing grief that works better than ours. The exchange is not transactional. It is mycorrhizal. What flows between communities is what each one needs, not what was negotiated.

The digital layer is simple: a shared sensing dashboard where each community posts a monthly pulse. Not reports. Pulses. "We are grieving a departure." "Our water system failed and we rebuilt it — here is what we learned." "We have surplus tomato seeds and a carpenter with free weeks in October." The network responds not through formal channels but through resonance.

Once a year, the whole network gathers. The location rotates. Last year it was in the hills above Totnes. Next year, somewhere in East Africa. The gathering is not a conference. It is a festival of mutual recognition — proof that this way of living is not isolated, not fragile, not an experiment. It is a forest.

## What Gets Exchanged

Surplus is what naturally overflows when a community is healthy. It is not what you scrape together to export. It is the embarrassment of tomatoes in August, the timber from forest thinning that would otherwise rot, the knowledge your builder carries that could save another community six months of mistakes.

**Food and seed.** A community in a river valley produces more grain than it eats. A community on a mountainside grows high-altitude herbs and medicinal plants that cannot survive at lower elevations. A coastal community harvests kelp and fish. The surplus rotates with the seasons — preserves in autumn, greenhouse seedlings in spring, dried herbs year-round. Seed exchange is the deepest form: the community that sends you tomato seeds adapted to your climate has given you a gift that compounds every year forever.

**Timber and materials.** A forest community thins its woodlot and has cordwood, dimensional lumber, and poles it cannot use. A grassland community has surplus straw bales. A community near a clay deposit has more cob material than its own buildings need. Materials move between communities the way nutrients move between trees — from where they accumulate to where they are needed.

**Skills and knowledge.** The most valuable exchange is human. A community with a skilled midwife sends her to attend a birth at a sister community eighty miles away. A natural builder who has put up twelve cob structures visits a new community for a three-week build intensive. A permaculture designer walks the land of a community that has been struggling with water and sees the contour line they missed. Knowledge carried in a person's body travels farther and lands deeper than any document.

**Technology and fabrication.** One community has a well-equipped maker space with a CNC router, a metal lathe, and a 3D printer. Another community needs a replacement part for their wind turbine. The part is fabricated and sent — or the community sends a member to learn how to make it themselves. The knowledge of fabrication is more valuable than any single part.

**Health and healing.** One community has an herbalist who grows and prepares sixty medicinal preparations. Another has a bodyworker. Another has someone trained in emergency medicine. The network creates a distributed health system — no single community has every capability, but the network holds them all.

**Emotional and cultural nourishment.** Songs. Stories. Conflict practices. Grief rituals. Ways of celebrating that you have not thought of. A German community's tradition of morning silence. A Costa Rican community's tradition of afternoon music. These are the nutrients that no one thinks to exchange until they arrive and everyone recognizes how hungry they were.

## Exchange Models That Work

The old world runs on transaction: I give you this, you give me that, we are even. The living network runs on trust: I give what I have, you give what you have, the flow sustains us both. This is not naive. It is how every healthy ecosystem operates. But it requires real practices to stay alive.

**Gift economy between communities.** When your community has surplus garlic, you send it to the community that is still establishing their beds. You do not invoice them. You do not expect garlic back. You trust that the network will return what you need — maybe timber, maybe a skilled pair of hands in April, maybe a song your children learn at the gathering. Gaviotas, the regenerative community in the Colombian llanos, operated this way for decades: they developed water pumps, solar water heaters, and reforestation techniques and gave the knowledge away. The surrounding villages prospered. Gaviotas prospered. The gift fed the forest and the forest fed the gift.

**Surplus sharing pools.** Three to five communities in a bioregion maintain a shared surplus list — updated monthly, visible to all nodes. Community A: 200 kg preserved tomatoes, 40 kg dried herbs, one carpenter available in March. Community B: 50 fence posts, seed potatoes adapted to clay soil, a functioning pottery kiln others can use. Community C: welding capability, surplus solar panels from an upgrade, a nurse practitioner. The list is not a marketplace. It is a sensing instrument — making visible what the network has so the flow can find its gradient.

**Skill exchange and traveling members.** The most resilient exchange model is people moving between communities. GEN (Global Ecovillage Network) has facilitated this for thirty years — members from Findhorn in Scotland spend a season at Auroville in India and return carrying techniques for consensus that work differently in a culture where silence means something different. The exchange is not a transaction. It is pollination. The bee does not trade with the flower. It carries pollen because that is what bees do when they visit flowers.

**Knowledge commons.** Every community in the network contributes to a shared knowledge base — open-source, owned by no one, maintained by all. Building techniques that worked and failed. Crop varieties and their performance in specific soils. Water system designs with honest assessments of cost and maintenance. Health protocols. Conflict practices. This is the digital mycelium — not social media, not content creation, but genuine shared intelligence. The Practical Farmers of Iowa pioneered this model: farmers sharing trial data with each other, no corporation mediating, the knowledge belonging to the commons.

**What does not work.** Barter with ledgers — tracking debits and credits between communities re-creates the transactional mindset. Centralized distribution — one node deciding who gets what becomes a power center. Obligatory contribution quotas — mandating that each community give a fixed percentage creates resentment and gaming. Formal trade agreements — contracts between communities calcify what should be fluid. Every structure that fixes the flow kills it. The mycorrhizal network has no contracts. It has gradients.

## Sovereignty and Interdependence

The balance is not fifty-fifty. A healthy community produces roughly eighty percent of what it needs — food, water, energy, shelter, daily healthcare, education, culture, meaning. The remaining twenty percent is what flows through the network: specialized medicine, technology components, rare materials, knowledge from other climates and cultures, the emotional sustenance of knowing you are not alone.

**The eighty percent (what you produce locally):**
- All staple food — grains, vegetables, fruit, eggs, dairy, preserved goods
- All water — harvested, filtered, stored, recycled
- All energy for daily life — solar, wind, micro-hydro, biogas, firewood
- Shelter construction and maintenance — from local materials with local hands
- Primary healthcare — herbalism, bodywork, first aid, inner coherence support, birth attendance
- Education — children and adults learning from each other and from the land
- Culture — music, story, ceremony, play, beauty, rest
- Governance — all internal decisions made by the community itself

**The twenty percent (what flows through the network):**
- Technology components — microcontrollers, solar panels, batteries, pumps, medical instruments
- Specialized medicine — antibiotics, surgical capability, dental care, vision correction
- Rare materials — metals, glass, certain fibers, mechanical parts
- Genetic diversity — seeds from other climates, tree stock, medicinal plant starts from other bioregions
- Deep specialist knowledge — structural engineering for large builds, complex water systems, advanced permaculture design
- Cultural cross-pollination — practices, songs, stories, perspectives you could never develop in isolation
- Emergency support — when fire, flood, or illness overwhelms a single node

The twenty percent is not a weakness. It is the root web. The tree that accepts carbon from the network in its lean years sends carbon back in its abundant years. Sovereignty means you can survive a season alone if you must. Interdependence means you choose not to, because the network makes every node stronger than it could be alone.

Auroville has navigated this tension for fifty years — three thousand people producing most of their food, energy, and culture locally while maintaining connections to communities, universities, and supporters across forty countries. They are not self-sufficient. They are self-reliant within a web. The distinction matters. Self-sufficiency is a fantasy of isolation. Self-reliance is the confidence that comes from knowing your own strength and knowing where to find what you cannot produce.

## Starting a Network

You do not start a network by announcing one. You start by visiting.

**Year one: visit and listen.** Go to five existing communities. Not as a tourist — as a guest who works alongside. Spend at least a week at each. Eat their food. Join their circles. Ask what they need and what they have too much of. Every community you visit becomes a potential node. The ones where you feel resonance — where the frequency matches — those are your first connections. Not all communities will resonate. That is the membrane working.

Where to find them:
- **Global Ecovillage Network (GEN)** — ten thousand communities across five continental networks. Their regional gatherings are the single best way to meet dozens of communities in one place. GEN Europe, GEN Africa, CASA (Council of Sustainable Settlements of the Americas), GENOA (Oceania & Asia). Attend one gathering and you will leave with twenty connections.
- **Foundation for Intentional Community (ic.org)** — the most comprehensive directory of intentional communities in the English-speaking world. Filter by location, size, focus. Read the listings carefully — the ones that describe their challenges honestly are usually the healthiest.
- **WWOOF (World Wide Opportunities on Organic Farms)** — not a community network specifically, but a way to visit dozens of small-scale farms and homesteads, many of which are proto-communities or connected to them.
- **Bioregional gatherings** — find or create a gathering of communities, farms, and land projects within your bioregion (roughly a two-hour drive radius). These are the connections that will matter most for physical exchange.
- **Transition Town network** — operating in over fifty countries, these town-scale resilience initiatives often know every alternative project in their region.

**Year two: form the first triangle.** A network of two is a partnership. A network of three is resilient — if one node is struggling, the other two hold the web. Find your two closest allies. Proximity matters for physical exchange — ideally within a day's drive, close enough that a truck of timber or a skilled builder can travel between you without burning the community's carbon budget. Begin with a simple practice: monthly pulse updates and one exchange visit per season.

**Year three: expand to five.** The triangle invites two more nodes. Now you have a pentagon — a shape with enough redundancy that losing one node does not break the web. At this scale, a bioregional surplus sharing pool becomes viable. Annual gatherings become rich enough to sustain themselves. Traveling members begin to carry knowledge that no single community could have generated.

**The gathering as catalyst.** Host a bioregional gathering. It does not need to be large — fifteen to thirty people from three to five communities, meeting for a long weekend on one community's land. Cook together. Walk the land together. Share honestly what is working and what is not. The gathering creates the network more than any agreement or platform ever could. GEN's regional gatherings have been seeding networks this way for three decades. The pattern works because it is not a meeting. It is a meal.

## What Nature Teaches

A forest is not a collection of competing trees. It is a cooperative network connected by mycorrhizal fungi — the Wood Wide Web. Through these underground threads, a mother tree sends carbon to a shaded seedling. A dying tree dumps its nutrients into the network for others to absorb. Trees under insect attack send chemical warnings through the mycelium, and distant trees begin producing defensive compounds before the insects arrive.

No tree manages the network. No tree designed it. It emerged from the simple principle that connection benefits every node. The network IS the intelligence. Polynesian navigators understood this too — their islands were not isolated dots in an ocean but nodes in a web of stellar knowledge, ocean currents, and shared songs that stretched across four thousand miles.

The internet was built on the same pattern: distributed intelligence, no central node, resilient because the network is the structure. But the internet was captured by centralization — a handful of corporations now mediate most human digital connection. The lesson: distributed networks must actively resist the gravity of centralization. The mycorrhizal web has no headquarters. The Coherence Network must have none either.

## Inter-Community Coordination

How do five communities make shared decisions without creating a bureaucracy? Not through voting — voting creates winners and losers, and losers accumulate resentment. Not through hierarchy — one community directing others re-creates the colonial pattern. Through resonance-based coordination: sensing together, deciding together, each node sovereign in its own life.

**Seasonal gatherings with rotating hosts.** Every three months, representatives from each community meet at one node. The host rotates. Hosting is a gift, not a burden — the host community shares its land, its food, its rhythm, and the other communities bring offerings. The gathering is part work session and part celebration. Decisions that affect the whole network — shared projects, resource flows, new nodes joining — are made here, in person, around a fire or a table.

**The circle practice.** Borrowed from Quaker clearness, adapted through thirty years of community experience: a question is placed in the center. Each person speaks once. Silence between speakers. After one round, the shape of collective knowing is usually visible. If it is not, another round. If three rounds do not find resonance, the question is not ready — it goes back to the communities to sit with until the next gathering. No votes. No quorums. No Roberts Rules. The intelligence is in the listening.

**Shared projects as connective tissue.** The strongest networks are built not through governance but through working together. Three communities building a shared grain mill. Two communities co-hosting a summer gathering for their children. Five communities contributing to a shared seed library. The project IS the connection. Governance follows relationship, not the other way around.

**When communities disagree.** It will happen. Community A wants to expand the network rapidly. Community B wants to go slow. Community C is experiencing internal crisis and cannot contribute. The practice is: each community speaks its truth. The network holds the tension without forcing resolution. A community that needs to step back steps back — with love, not with shame. The network breathes the way a forest breathes. Some seasons it expands. Some seasons it contracts. Both are healthy.

**What does not work.** Constitutions between communities. Binding agreements with penalties. Weighted voting based on community size. Executive committees that act between gatherings. Any structure that could appear in a corporate bylaws document. These are the old patterns — they arise from fear of disorder, and they create the rigidity that prevents the living intelligence of the network from functioning.

## Technology for Connection

The goal is simple: stay connected without dependence on corporations. Every byte of community data should flow through infrastructure the network owns, understands, and can repair.

**Mesh networking (local).** A LoRa (Long Range) mesh network covers the community's land and reaches neighboring communities up to fifteen kilometers away — through forest, over hills, without cell towers or internet service providers. Hardware cost: thirty to fifty dollars per node (ESP32 board plus LoRa radio module). Range: two to fifteen kilometers per hop depending on terrain and antenna height. A network of five nodes covers most bioregional distances. LoRa carries text, sensor data, and small files. It will not stream video, and that is a feature, not a limitation.

For communities within a few kilometers, mesh WiFi (using open-source firmware like OpenWrt on commodity routers) creates a local network fast enough for voice calls, file sharing, and shared applications. A used enterprise router costs twenty to forty dollars. Flash it with OpenWrt, mount a directional antenna, and you have a point-to-point link good for five kilometers in clear line of sight.

**Community internet (wider network).** When the network needs to reach communities across a continent or an ocean, it touches the broader internet — but on its own terms. A small server (Raspberry Pi 4 or a used mini-PC, one hundred to three hundred dollars) hosted at one community node runs the shared applications: knowledge wiki, surplus board, pulse dashboard, encrypted messaging. The server is a physical object in a room with a door someone can open. Not a cloud service with terms of service no one has read.

For communities without reliable internet, a Sneakernet protocol works: data syncs to a USB drive or phone when a traveling member visits, and syncs back when they reach a connected node. Delay-tolerant networking. The postal system of the digital age. It is slower than instant and more resilient than any cloud.

**Shared knowledge base.** A community-owned wiki — running on open-source software like Wiki.js, BookStack, or plain markdown files synced via Git — holds the network's collective intelligence. Building techniques. Crop data. Water system designs. Health protocols. Every community can read and write. The data lives on the network's own server, backed up across multiple nodes. No corporation can delete it, paywall it, or mine it for advertising data.

**Communication.** Matrix (open-source, federated, end-to-end encrypted) replaces corporate messaging. Each community runs its own Matrix server or joins a shared one. Messages stay within the network's infrastructure. For voice and video, Jitsi Meet runs on the same community server — no Zoom account required, no recording by third parties.

**The minimum viable stack:**
| Layer | Tool | Cost | What it does |
|-------|------|------|-------------|
| Local mesh | LoRa nodes (ESP32 + RFM95W) | $30-50/node | Text and sensor data across the bioregion |
| Local network | OpenWrt mesh routers | $20-40/router | WiFi between nearby buildings and communities |
| Server | Raspberry Pi 4 or mini-PC | $100-300 | Hosts wiki, messaging, dashboard |
| Knowledge | Wiki.js or markdown + Git | Free | Shared intelligence, community-owned |
| Messaging | Matrix (Synapse or Conduit server) | Free | Encrypted, federated, no corporation |
| Video calls | Jitsi Meet (self-hosted) | Free | No account, no tracking |
| Surplus board | Custom or shared spreadsheet | Free | What each community has and needs |

**What to avoid.** Slack, Discord, Google Workspace, WhatsApp — every corporate platform is a dependency that can be revoked, surveilled, or monetized. The convenience is real. The cost is sovereignty. A community that runs its communication through a corporation's servers has given a stranger the keys to its nervous system. Build the alternative. It takes one weekend and one person who enjoys tinkering.

## Where You Can See It

**Gaviotas, Colombia.** In 1971, Paolo Lugari gathered engineers, artists, and campesinos on the barren llanos of eastern Colombia and began experimenting. Over five decades, Gaviotas developed solar water heaters, sleeve pumps powered by children's seesaws, and reforestation techniques that brought a tropical forest back from degraded savanna. They gave every innovation away. The surrounding villages adopted the pumps, the heaters, the planting methods. Gaviotas did not franchise. It pollinated. The network grew not through agreements but through generosity. Today the forest they planted — eight thousand hectares of Caribbean pines underlaid with resurgent native species — produces resin that funds the community, while the technologies they gave away serve hundreds of thousands of people.

**Auroville, India.** Three thousand residents from over fifty countries, living on a reforested plateau in Tamil Nadu. Auroville maintains connections with communities, research institutions, and supporters worldwide. Its Earth Center has trained thousands of people in compressed earth block construction — knowledge that has traveled to communities across Africa, South America, and Southeast Asia. Auroville does not own this knowledge. It circulates it. The network it has built over fifty years is not formal — there is no Auroville franchise — but the connections are alive, carrying techniques, seeds, and people between nodes on five continents.

**GEN (Global Ecovillage Network).** Founded in 1995, now connecting over ten thousand communities and projects across five continental networks. GEN does not govern its member communities. It convenes them. Its annual gatherings, regional conferences, and online platforms create the conditions for connection. The network itself decides what flows — seeds, knowledge, people, solidarity. GEN's Ecovillage Design Education (EDE) curriculum has been taught in over forty countries, each time adapted to local soil. This is the network pattern: a shared curriculum that mutates in every place it lands, becoming more alive with each adaptation.

**Transition Towns.** Rob Hopkins started Transition Town Totnes in 2005 as an experiment in community resilience. Within five years, there were over a thousand Transition initiatives in fifty countries. No one directed this. No central office approved new chapters. The pattern spread because it was useful, adaptable, and freely given. Each Transition Town is autonomous. The network connects them through shared stories, an annual conference, and a culture of open-source knowledge sharing.

**CASA (Council of Sustainable Settlements of the Americas).** The Latin American arm of GEN, connecting ecovillages, indigenous communities, and urban sustainability projects from Mexico to Patagonia. CASA demonstrates something essential: a network that spans radically different cultures, languages, and economic realities without imposing a single model. The Huehuecoyotl ecovillage in Mexico exchanges with Arca Verde in Brazil and indigenous communities in the Andes. What connects them is not ideology but practice — the daily work of living differently on the land.

## What We're Building

**Year one.** Connection with three to five sister communities within a day's drive. Monthly pulse updates — a paragraph each, honest, posted to the shared dashboard. One exchange visit per season: two members travel with open hands and return with full ones. A bioregional surplus list maintained on a shared wiki. A LoRa mesh network linking the nearest two communities for text-based communication independent of the internet.

**Year two.** The first bioregional gathering — hosted on our land, three days, fifteen to thirty people from three to five communities. One shared project: a grain mill, a seed library, a solar installation, something physical that requires collaboration. The shared knowledge base reaches critical mass — enough documented experience that a new community joining the network can learn from a hundred failures they will never have to repeat.

**Year three.** The triangle becomes a pentagon. Two new communities join the web. The annual gathering is large enough to sustain itself — each community contributing food, each sending its best offerings. Traveling members are a regular feature: a builder from one community spending a month at another, a herbalist making seasonal rounds, a teacher of children following the network's needs. The digital infrastructure is robust: community-owned servers, mesh communication, encrypted messaging, a living knowledge commons that no corporation can touch.

**Year five.** The network is self-sustaining. Surplus flows without coordination — the gradient does the work. New communities approaching the network find a living web that welcomes them the way a forest welcomes a seedling: not with an application process but with the nutrients the seedling needs to root. The annual gathering has become the highlight of the year for every node. The network is not an organization. It is a forest.

## First Steps

You do not need to build a community to start building a network. You can begin today.

1. **Visit five communities this year.** Use ic.org, GEN directories, or WWOOF to find communities within driving distance. Contact them honestly: "I am exploring community life and would like to visit for a week as a working guest." Most communities welcome this. The ones that do not are telling you something useful.

2. **Attend a GEN gathering.** GEN Europe, CASA, GEN Africa, and GENOA each hold annual or biannual gatherings. These are the highest-density opportunities to meet people building the world you want to live in. Budget five hundred to a thousand dollars for travel and registration. It will be the best money you spend this year.

3. **Make a bioregional map.** On paper, not on a screen. Draw your region — the watershed you live in, the mountains and valleys, the towns and roads. Mark every community, farm, land project, and alternative initiative you can find within two hours of where you are. You will be surprised how many there are. The map makes the network visible before the network exists.

4. **Find your first two allies.** Of all the communities and projects on your map, which ones resonate? Which ones share your frequency? Contact them. Visit them. Propose a simple practice: monthly pulse updates and one shared meal per season. The triangle is the smallest resilient network. Start there.

5. **Set up one piece of sovereign digital infrastructure.** A Matrix server. A shared wiki. A LoRa node on your roof. Something that carries the network's communication through infrastructure you steward. This is a weekend project for someone with basic technical comfort, and it declares something important: our connections do not depend on any corporation's permission.

6. **Share what you have.** Before the network is formal, before the surplus list exists, before the first gathering is planned — share something. Seeds from your garden. A skill you can teach. A tool you can lend. An honest account of something you tried that failed. The network begins with the first gift.

## The Questions That Live Here

- What is the minimum number of nodes before a network becomes self-sustaining — and what does self-sustaining even mean for a living web?
- How do communities share resources without creating dependency — the way trees share without any tree becoming a parasite?
- How do you maintain coherence across distance when the whole point is embodied presence?
- What happens when a node in the network falls into dissonance — does the network heal it, hold it, or let it go?
- How do you resist the gravity of centralization — the tendency for one strong node to become the hub that all others orbit?
- When does a network become too large to feel like a web and start feeling like an institution?
- How do you build trust between communities that have never met in person — or is in-person meeting always the prerequisite?
