# The Network

> The mycorrhizal web between communities. No community is an island.
> Seeds, knowledge, people, celebration flowing between nodes.

## The Feeling

I am standing at the edge of a gathering
where three communities have converged.
The hosts are from a valley in Portugal. The visitors arrived yesterday
from a forest village in Germany and a coastal collective in Costa Rica.
Nobody is presenting. Nobody is networking.
People are cooking together, comparing fermentation techniques,
arguing gently about water catchment designs,
teaching each other songs in three languages.

A woman from the German village is showing our builder a timber joint
she learned from a Japanese carpenter who visited her community last spring.
The knowledge traveled: Japan to Germany to Portugal,
carried in hands and memory, not in a document.
A child from Costa Rica is playing with children
who don't share a single word of common language.
They don't need one. They have sticks, mud,
and the universal grammar of play.

I feel something in my chest that I can only describe
as belonging to something larger. Not an organization. Not a movement.
A living network — the way roots belong to a forest.

## How It Lives Here

Three times a year, two or three of our members travel to a sister community.
They stay for two weeks. They work alongside their hosts,
eat their food, learn their rhythms.
When they return, they carry something back — a seed variety,
a governance practice, a song, a way of processing grief
that works better than ours. The exchange is not transactional.
It is mycorrhizal. What flows between communities
is what each one needs, not what was negotiated.

The digital layer is simple: a shared sensing dashboard
where each community posts a monthly pulse. Not reports. Pulses.
"We are grieving a departure."
"Our water system failed and we rebuilt it — here is what we learned."
"We have surplus tomato seeds and a carpenter with free weeks in October."
The network responds not through formal channels but through resonance.

Once a year, the whole network gathers. The location rotates.
Last year it was in the hills above Totnes. Next year, somewhere in East Africa.
The gathering is not a conference. It is a festival of mutual recognition —
proof that this way of living is not isolated, not fragile, not an experiment.
It is a forest.

## What Nature Teaches

A forest is not a collection of competing trees.
It is a cooperative network connected by mycorrhizal fungi — the Wood Wide Web.
Through these underground threads, a mother tree sends carbon to a shaded seedling.
A dying tree dumps its nutrients into the network for others to absorb.
Trees under insect attack send chemical warnings through the mycelium,
and distant trees begin producing defensive compounds before the insects arrive.

No tree manages the network. No tree designed it.
It emerged from the simple principle that connection benefits every node.
The network IS the intelligence. Polynesian navigators understood this too —
their islands were not isolated dots in an ocean but nodes in a web
of stellar knowledge, ocean currents, and shared songs.

The internet was built on the same pattern: distributed intelligence,
no central node, resilient because the network is the structure.
The Coherence Network is this at the scale of human community.
Not organized. Networked.

## Where You Can See It

Walk into any Global Ecovillage Network gathering and feel it —
ten thousand communities connected by shared questions, not shared answers.
Visit Transition Towns from Totnes to Tokyo,
each one adapting the same resilience patterns to wildly different soil.
Find the New Earth communities building sovereign territory in Africa,
connected to sister projects on every continent.

These are not branches of an organization.
They are nodes in a living web,
each unique, the network greater than any node.

## What We're Building

Connection with three to five sister communities in the first year.
Quarterly exchange visits where members travel with open hands
and return with full ones.
A shared digital sensing layer — simple, honest, human.
An annual gathering that rotates through the network,
so every node eventually hosts the whole.
Not an organization with chapters. A forest with roots.

## The Questions That Live Here

- What is the minimum number of nodes before a network becomes self-sustaining?
- How do communities share resources without creating dependency — the way trees share without any tree becoming a parasite?
- How do you maintain coherence across distance when the whole point is embodied presence?
- What happens when a node in the network falls into dysfunction — does the network heal it, hold it, or let it go?
