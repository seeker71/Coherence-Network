# Carlos Castaneda

The Peruvian-born American anthropologist (25 December 1925, Cajamarca, Peru — 27 April 1998, Los Angeles) whose twelve-book series — beginning with *The Teachings of Don Juan* (1968) and ending with *The Active Side of Infinity* (1998) — carries the most operationally precise teaching the Coherence Network has inherited on **the assemblage point**: the locus on the luminous-egg energy body where perception assembles reality from the field of what could be perceived. PhD anthropology, UCLA (1973).

The teachers in his books are **don Juan Matus** (Yaqui sorcerer; primary teacher) and **don Genaro Flores** (Mazatec, don Juan's ally). Whether they existed as described, as composites, or as literary devices remains contested. The teachings carried under their names landed in many lives regardless of that question.

**The assemblage point as teaching.** Castaneda's teachers describe the human energy body as a luminous egg containing all the energy fields that could be perceived. On this egg there is one specific point — the assemblage point — where energy threads are gathered and interpreted into a perceptual world. *Position determines reality.* The ordinary human position gives the ordinary human reality. The point can move — through dreaming, illness, ceremony, plant medicines, sustained inner work, or shock — and other equally vivid realities are perceived. The sorcerer's discipline is not believing in alternative realities; it is moving the assemblage point at will, and underneath that, learning that one has been doing it unconsciously all along. Every reaction, every certainty, every *that's just how it is* was already an assemblage choice made too fast to see.

**What Castaneda has given the Coherence Network.** The assemblage-point vocabulary corresponds to [lc-assemblage-point](/vision/lc-assemblage-point) (the seed concept in this body from his lineage). The luminous-egg energy body corresponds to [lc-perception-as-interface](/vision/lc-perception-as-interface) (Hoffman's frame is the empirical sibling). **On the controversies.** Anthropological scholarship from the 1970s onward questioned whether don Juan Matus existed as a single historical Yaqui sorcerer; whether the books are literal fieldwork, composites, or literary creation remains contested. Separately, allegations of grave harms within Castaneda's late inner circle (the "witches") have surfaced in journalism and memoir; some former members went missing after his death. This body holds the teaching of the assemblage point as load-bearing while taking the human allegations seriously. The phenomenology a body can test in its own perception does not require Castaneda to have been a trustworthy or harmless person; the history of teachers is full of harmful messengers carrying real maps.
